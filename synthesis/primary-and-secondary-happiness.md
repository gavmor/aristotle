# Primary vs. Secondary Happiness (Theōria vs. Moral Praxis)

In Book X, Chapters 7–8 of the *Nicomachean Ethics*, Aristotle brings his inquiry to a climax by establishing a strict two-tiered hierarchy of human flourishing (*eudaimonia*): **Primary Happiness** found in theoretical contemplation (*theōria*), and **Secondary Happiness** (*deuterōs*) found in the active life of moral virtue and practical judgment (*phronēsis*).

## Comparative Structure

| Criterion | Primary Happiness (*Theōria*) | Secondary Happiness (Moral *Praxis*) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Governing Faculty** | Divine Intellect (*Nous*) | Compound Nature (*Syntheton*: Reason + Passions) |
| **Primary Virtue** | Theoretical Wisdom (*Sophia*) | Virtues of Character & Practical Judgment (*Phronēsis*) |
| **Domain** | Eternal, Unchanging Truths (*Incapable of being otherwise*) | Variable Human & Civic Affairs (*Capable of being otherwise*) |
| **Self-Sufficiency (*Autarkeia*)** | Highest — requires minimal external equipment | Lower — requires wealth, power, body, and social sphere |
| **Leisure (*Scholē*)** | Loved purely for its own sake; contains inherent leisure | Aimed at peace/order; practiced in busy civic life (*ascholia*) |
| **Relation to the Divine** | Direct participation in divine activity; most dear to the gods | Distinctly human concerns (*anthrōpika*) |

## Key Insights

- **The Dual Nature of Man:** Human beings are composite creatures (*syntheton*), occupying the boundary between mortal animal life and divine intellect. Secondary happiness is the peak of our mortal/social nature, while primary happiness is the realization of the divine spark (*theion*) within us. ^[extracted]
- **The Self-Sufficiency Differential:** A moral act requires external "equipment" (*organa*)—a generous person needs money, a courageous person needs physical strength/opportunity, a just person needs transaction partners. A contemplative person needs none of these to engage in contemplation (*theōria*), though as a human living among others, they still require basic health and sustenance. ^[extracted]
- **Integration of the Treatise:** Book I defined happiness broadly as "being-at-work of the soul in accordance with complete virtue," while Book X narrows *complete* happiness (*teleia eudaimonia*) to contemplation, leaving moral virtue as an essential, indispensable foundation that makes human social life orderly and choiceworthy. ^[inferred]

### Context & Morphological Notes

* **`δευτέρως` (*deuterōs*):** Adverb formed from the ordinal numeral **`δεύτερος`** (*deuteros*, "second"). It explicitly assigns second-tier rank to moral/political virtue compared to theoretical contemplation (*theōria*).
* **`ἀνθρωπικαί` (*anthrōpikai*):** Nominative feminine plural adjective from **`ἀνθρωπικός`** (*anthrōpikos*, "belonging to a human being / mortal-scaled"). Aristotle contrasts these distinctly human activities—bound up with bodily passions, social transactions, and compound nature (*syntheton*)—with the divine (*theion*) nature of theoretical intellect (*nous*).

Nicomachean Ethics

Aristotle's ten-book inquiry into human happiness (eudaimonia) — what it is, what it depends on, and how virtue of character and virtue of thought together constitute it. This page distills the Joe Sachs translation (Focus Publishing, 2002), whose introduction and footnotes emphasize recovering Aristotle's original Greek vocabulary against a Latin-tradition translation history that (per Sachs) has systematically flattened its key terms.

Overview

The inquiry opens by asking what the highest human good is, and answers via the "ergon argument": since a flute-player's good work is playing the flute well, and the good of anything is found in its characteristic activity done well, the good of a human being must be a being-at-work of the soul in accordance with reason — and if there is more than one virtue, in accordance with the best and most complete one, over a complete life. This is happiness.

Books II–V work out virtue of character as an active condition acquired by habituation, structured as a mean between excess and deficiency, chosen for the sake of the beautiful, and voluntary in a sense clarified through the analysis of choice (Book III) and worked out in detail for justice (Book V). Book VI turns to virtue of thought, distinguishing practical judgment from theoretical wisdom and arguing the two kinds of virtue are mutually dependent. Book VII examines lack of self-restraint as a condition distinct from both vice and virtue, and reopens the question of pleasure. Books VIII–IX are the Ethics' longest sustained discussion, on friendship, including the striking claim that a friend is "another self." Book X completes the treatment of pleasure (as unimpeded activity, not process or restoration) before arguing that complete happiness is contemplation — the being-at-work of the most divine part in us — with the life of moral virtue and practical action ranking as happiness "in a secondary way." The work closes by explicitly handing off to the Politics, since laws and habituation, not argument alone, are what actually make people good.

Key Concepts

  • concepts/eudaimonia — happiness as complete, self-sufficient being-at-work of the soul in accordance with virtue
  • concepts/energeia — being-at-work, Aristotle's central ontological term, distinguished from mere motion or process
  • concepts/hexis — active condition/character, acquired by habituation, the genus of virtue and vice
  • concepts/doctrine-of-the-mean — virtue of character as a mean between vices of excess and deficiency
  • concepts/to-kalon — the beautiful, the end for the sake of which virtuous action is chosen
  • concepts/prohairesis — choice, and the analysis of voluntary/involuntary action underlying moral responsibility
  • concepts/phronesis — practical judgment, the intellectual virtue inseparable from good character
  • concepts/akrasia — lack of self-restraint, distinct in kind from vice
  • concepts/justice-nicomachean — justice as complete virtue toward others, natural vs. conventional justice, and equity
  • concepts/distributive-justice — shares of common goods by geometric proportion
  • concepts/corrective-justice — transactions, reciprocity, and currency by arithmetic proportion
  • concepts/philia — friendship, in three species (utility, pleasure, virtue)
  • concepts/pleasure-aristotle — pleasure as the unimpeded activity of a natural capacity, not a process of becoming
  • concepts/contemplative-life — theoretical contemplation as complete happiness

Entities

  • entities/aristotle — the author
  • entities/joe-sachs — the translator, whose introduction reframes hexis, energeia, and to kalon against the standard Latinate renderings ("habit," "activity," "noble")

Open Questions

  • The relation between the "complete happiness" of Book X (contemplation) and the "secondary happiness" of moral/political virtue is left in tension: contemplation needs almost no external goods, but the moral virtues need a great deal, and the work never fully reconciles which life an actual human being (as opposed to "the divine element in us") should live. ^[inferred]
  • Sachs's introduction argues that friendship, not justice, is the Ethics' real answer to how political community should cohere — justice being treated as "inherently inadequate" and superseded by the discussion of friendship in Books VIII–IX. This is Sachs's interpretive claim rather than a thesis Aristotle states outright. ^[ambiguous]

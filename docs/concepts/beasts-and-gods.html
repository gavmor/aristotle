Brutishness and Superhuman Virtue

Book VII opens by naming "three kinds of things to be avoided that have to do with one's character: vice, lack of self-restraint, and an animal-like state." Vice and unrestraint get the book's sustained attention, but the third term — brutishness (theriotes) — and its opposite, a virtue "that transcends us," are named here as a pair standing outside the ordinary vice/virtue scale altogether, not as further points along it.

Key Ideas

  • Both extremes lie off the human scale entirely. "Just as there is no vice or virtue belonging to an animal, so too neither state belongs to a god" — brutishness and superhuman virtue are not simply more-vicious-than-vice or more-virtuous-than-virtue; Aristotle treats them as categorically different in kind from ordinary character, the way an animal's nature and a god's nature both differ in kind from a human's. Superhuman virtue is "something more honorable than virtue," and brutishness "something of a different kind from vice," not merely a further degree of either. ^[extracted]
  • Superhuman/heroic virtue is named from Homer: Priam calling Hector godlike, "not the child of a mortal man but of a god" — the Spartans' own idiom for someone admired "exceedingly" is to call him a "gawdlike man." Aristotle treats this as a real, if extremely rare, human possibility: "if... people are turned from humans into gods by a surpassing degree of virtue," the opposite pole of the animal-like state must be pitched at that same transcendent register. ^[extracted]
  • Brutishness is rare among humans and, tellingly, most associated with "the barbarians" or with people whose condition results from "diseases or defects" — Aristotle's own example of the concept in ordinary use is looser and more familiar: "we also disparage people who surpass the rest of human beings in vice by calling them animal-like," the way extreme cruelty gets called "inhuman" or "monstrous" in English, gesturing at something beyond ordinary bad character. ^[inferred]
  • This pair frames, but doesn't compete with, the book's real subject. Aristotle explicitly defers full treatment of brutishness ("about this sort of disposition, one must make some mention later") and pivots immediately to the actual topic of Book VII: unrestraint, self-restraint, softness, and endurance, which "must not" be assumed to belong to "the same active conditions as virtue and vice" either — a third, intermediate way of missing or hitting the mark, distinct from both the vice/virtue axis and the beast/god axis. ^[extracted]

"For brutishness, it would be most fitting to speak of a virtue beyond us, a heroic and divine one — the way Homer has Priam say of Hector that he was exceedingly good: 'nor did he seem to be the child of a mortal man, but of a god.'" "Since it is rare for a godlike man to exist — as the Spartans customarily say, when they greatly admire someone, 'a godlike man' — so too the brutish person is rare among human beings; it is found most among the barbarians, and some cases arise from disease or defect; and we likewise disparage as 'brutish' those who surpass other people in vice." This backs the page's third bullet: brutishness is rare and marginal — most associated with foreigners or with disease/defect, not a common human failing. Barbaros (βαρ- imitative babble-syllable, reduplicated + -ος) literally means "one who babbles" — a non-Greek speaker — so the word's own etymology already carries the "outsider" charge the passage trades on.

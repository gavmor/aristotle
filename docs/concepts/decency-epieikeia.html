Decency (Epieikeia)

Bk. V, ch. 10 takes up epieikeia — "usually translated in this chapter as 'equity' in its technical legal sense," a footnote in this edition notes, but "this is misleading." Translator Joe Sachs renders it "decency" instead, joining hexis, energeia, and to kalon as another deliberate departure from the standard English tradition: the glossary entry is explicit that Aristotle "is describing something that goes beyond what is equitable (to ison)" — decency isn't a technical legal-equity doctrine but one of "Aristotle's most frequent ways of naming human goodness."

Key Ideas

  • The puzzle decency resolves: is what's decent the same as what's just, or different? Aristotle says both intuitions are partly right — "the decent thing, though it is better than a certain kind of just thing, is just, and is not better than what is just by being of some other kind." Decency isn't a rival standard to justice; it's a correction internal to justice, applied when strict legal justice, precisely because it's stated universally, produces the wrong answer in a particular case. ^[extracted]
  • The reason lies in what a law necessarily is: "every law is universal, and there are some things about which it is not possible to speak rightly when speaking universally." A law "takes in what applies to the greater number of cases, not failing to recognize the error" — the lawmaker isn't at fault, and neither is the law; "the error is not in the law or in the lawmaker, but in the nature of a particular case," since "the material of which actions consist is that way straight from the start." ^[extracted]
  • The Lesbian ruler: "a rule for something indeterminate is also indeterminate, as is the lead straightedge used in housebuilding on Lesbos; for the ruler is altered to fit the shape of the stone and does not stay rigid." Decency is this ruler applied to law: not abandoning the law's aim, but bending the letter of it back to what "the lawmaker himself would say if he were there," had he foreseen the case. ^[extracted]
  • A decent person is defined by a specific, almost counterintuitive habit: someone "inclined to choose and to do things of that sort," who is "not rigidly precise about justice to a fault, but is inclined to take less, even when he has the support of the law." Decency, in other words, shows up as a disposition to forgo a strict legal entitlement, not merely as clever legal interpretation. ^[extracted]
  • This sits one level above the courtroom machinery already documented elsewhere: where corrective justice supplies the mechanism for setting transactions right by an equal, arithmetic mean, decency supplies the standard for when the letter of that mechanism should yield to what the case actually calls for — the same "setting straight" language Aristotle uses for corrective justice itself (diorthoun) recurs here: decency is called "a setting straight of what is legally just." ^[extracted]

This is the sentence that opens the chapter and frames the whole puzzle the page's first bullet describes; note that Aristotle's own opening move already treats epieikeia (the trait) and dikaiosynē (justice) as two nouns to be measured against each other, not as competitors in the same genus.

"For the decent, being a certain kind of just, is better than just, and it is not as being some other kind that it is better than the just." This is the sentence the page's first bullet paraphrases directly — decency resolves the puzzle by being better than a certain kind of just thing while remaining just, and the twice-repeated participle on ("being") pins the decent down as still-just at the very moment it is called better.

"What creates the puzzle is that the decent is just, but not the just according to law — rather, it is a correction of legally-just." The word carrying the page's fifth bullet is epanorthōma: epi- ("in addition") plus an- ("back, again") plus the orth- root of orthos ("straight, right") plus the result-noun suffix -ma — literally "a further straightening-back," the same orth- root the page already connects to corrective justice's diorthoun.

"The cause is that law is entirely universal, but about some things it is not possible to speak rightly in universal terms." This is the sentence behind the page's second bullet on "every law is universal"; katholou — kath- ("throughout, according to") fused to hol- (the "whole" of holos, source of English "holistic" and "catholic") plus an adverbial ending — names the very wholesale-ness of law that produces the gap decency has to fill, and Aristotle repeats the word at both ends of the sentence to underline it.

"For of the indeterminate the rule too is indeterminate, just as the leaden rule of Lesbian building also is; for it shifts to fit the shape of the stone and the rule does not stay fixed, and so too the decree in relation to the facts." This is the sentence behind the page's third bullet, the Lesbian ruler; aoristos — a-privative plus the root of horizein ("to bound, mark a boundary," source of English "horizon") plus a verbal-adjective suffix — names the matter of action as literally boundary-less, which is why the kanōn ("rule," here the flexible lead straightedge) has to bend to the stone rather than the reverse.

"The one who is not precisely-just toward the worse but inclined to take less, even though he has the law as his supporter, is decent, and this disposition is decency, being a certain justice and not some other disposition." This is the sentence the page's fourth bullet turns on; elattōtikos — the comparative stem elatt- ("less, smaller") plus a verb-forming element plus the -tikos suffix marking a standing tendency — builds the disposition to forgo a legal entitlement directly into the word's own ending, rather than describing it as sharper legal reasoning.

Self-Love (Philautia)

Book IX, ch. 8 takes up a puzzle generated by the friendship books themselves: all the marks of a friend — wishing someone well for their own sake, spending time with them, sharing their joys and pains — "arise out of oneself and extend to others," so that "one is a friend to oneself most of all." Yet "self-lover" is an ordinary term of reproach. Aristotle resolves the tension by arguing the word is used in two genuinely different senses, not one.

Key Ideas

  • The reproached sense: someone who "distribute[s] a greater amount to himself in the way of money, honors, and bodily pleasures" — gratifying "the irrational part of the soul." Aristotle grants this is the majority use of the word, and that it deserves the reproach it gets, "since most people are of this kind." ^[extracted]
  • The good sense, discovered by asking what "oneself" really refers to: just as a city is most truly identified with its most authoritative part, a human being is most truly identified with intellect — "the things people seem most of all to have done themselves and willingly are the things they have done with reason." Someone who "gratifies what is most authoritative in himself" — who takes for himself the most beautiful and good actions, at whatever cost to money, honor, or comfort — is a self-lover in a wholly different, praiseworthy sense: "differing as much as living by reason does from living by passion." ^[extracted]
  • A decent person's self-love and other-directedness turn out not to compete. Because what he loves most is his own capacity to act beautifully, a good person will give away money, honors, and even actions themselves to friends — "it would be a more beautiful thing to become responsible for the friend's performing them than to perform them himself" — since securing the beautiful for himself is what his self-love actually consists in, and beautiful action toward friends counts as much as beautiful action anywhere else. He therefore "distributes to himself the greater share of the beautiful" precisely by appearing to sacrifice the ordinary goods (money, honor, comfort) that the reproached self-lover hoards. ^[extracted]
  • This directly resolves rather than sidesteps the reproach: "a good person ought to be a lover of self, since he will both profit himself and benefit others... a corrupt person ought not, since he will harm himself and those around him." Self-love isn't rejected as a category; it's split into a form that damages the community and a form that is, in the end, identical with virtue itself. ^[extracted]

So the good man ought to be a self-lover (for by doing beautiful things he will benefit himself and help others too), but the base man ought not — for by following base passions he will harm both himself and those near him. The chapter's verdict lands squarely on φίλαυτον itself (φίλ-, root of philos, "dear, loving," fused to αὐτ-, "self," plus an adjectival ending): the very word under dispute divides cleanly along the line of virtue, which is exactly the resolution the page's fourth bullet reports.

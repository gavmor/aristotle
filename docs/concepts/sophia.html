Sophia (Theoretical Wisdom)

Book VI, ch. 7 defines sophia as "knowledge and intellect of the things that are most honorable in their nature" — it "must be the most precise form of knowledge," combining intellect's grasp of unprovable first principles with the demonstrative reasoning of scientific knowledge (episteme) built on top of them. Aristotle's example of the word's ordinary use is telling: we call Phidias a "wise" sculptor, meaning nothing more than that he is the most precise and accomplished at his art — but sophia in the unqualified sense belongs to someone "wise wholly, and not in part."

Key Ideas

  • Sophia and practical judgment are both intellectual virtues, but point in opposite directions. Practical judgment is concerned with what is "good and advantageous for a human being" and with action, which is always about particular, changeable things; sophia is concerned with what is unchanging and universal — the most honorable objects of study, not the most useful. This is why Aristotle can raise a real impasse (ch. 12) about sophia's use: "wisdom contemplates nothing by which a human being will be happy, since it is not directed at any form of coming into being." ^[extracted]
  • Aristotle's provisional answer to that impasse: wisdom "produces happiness... by being possessed and being at work," the way health produces health rather than the way medicine produces health — sophia doesn't make anything happen instrumentally, it simply is, in its own exercise, the activity that (at least in part) constitutes a good human life. This anticipates, without yet fully arguing, Book X's much stronger claim. ^[extracted]
  • Book X ch. 7-8 escalates sophia from "one of the virtues" to the candidate for complete happiness: contemplation (theoria) — sophia's characteristic activity — is "the most complete, most continuous, most self-sufficient being-at-work available to a human being," and therefore complete happiness itself, on the grounds that it is the activity of what is "most divine" in us and needs the fewest external conditions to be exercised. ^[extracted]
  • This creates the tension already flagged on concepts/eudaimonia and concepts/contemplative-life: if sophia/contemplation is complete happiness, practical judgment and the virtues of character built around it (justice, courage, friendship) risk looking like second-tier, merely human happiness by comparison — a hierarchy Aristotle himself seems uneasy about, given how much of the rest of the book is devoted to the practical virtues. ^[inferred]

"So wisdom would be intellect plus scientific knowledge, like knowledge that has a head on it, of the most honorable things." This is the sentence behind the opening paragraph's claim that sophia combines nous with epistēmē: ἐπιστήμη (epistēmē) is built from epi- "upon" + stē-, the root of histēmi "to stand," + the abstract-noun suffix -mē — literally a "standing-upon," i.e. knowledge secure enough to stand on a demonstrated foundation, which is exactly why it needs nous underneath it to grasp the undemonstrable first principles it stands upon.

This is the sentence behind the first bullet's stated impasse: the verb θεωρήσει (theōrēsei) — future tense of theōrein, "to look at, behold, contemplate" — names sophia's one and only activity, and Aristotle's worry is that beholding is not a making, so on its face it cannot produce anything, happiness included.

"Whether this itself is something divine, or whether it is the most divine thing in us, its being-at-work in accordance with its own virtue would be complete happiness." This is the sentence behind the third and fourth bullets' escalation: θειότατον (theiotaton) is the superlative of theos "god" (root thei- + superlative ending -otaton), and it is this single word — "most divine" — that Aristotle uses as his warrant for promoting contemplation's being-at-work to complete happiness, the very move that makes the practical virtues look second-tier by comparison.

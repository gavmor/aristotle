Aristotle

Ancient Greek philosopher, student of Plato at the Academy, later tutor to Alexander the Great and founder of the Lyceum. The references/nicomachean-ethics is his major work on ethics, so named (per the translator's note) most likely because it was edited by or dedicated to his son Nicomachus.

Key Ideas

  • Studied under Plato but broke with him on the theory of Forms, arguing there is no separable "good itself" common to all good things by one form — good is said in as many senses as being itself is said. ^[extracted]
  • Treats philosophy as beginning from received opinions (endoxa) and the things "known to us," working dialectically toward first principles, rather than deducing conclusions from first principles laid down in advance — a method he credits to Plato even while diverging from him. ^[extracted]
  • His central technical vocabulary — being-at-work (energeia), active condition (hexis), and the beautiful (to kalon) — recurs across his ethical, natural-philosophical, and metaphysical works, which is part of why translator Joe Sachs insists on consistent, non-Latinized renderings of these terms. ^[extracted]
  • Famous verse attributed to him calls Plato "a man whom the wicked have no right even to praise," reflecting the "friend to Plato, but a greater friend to the truth" principle that governs his disagreements with his teacher. ^[extracted]

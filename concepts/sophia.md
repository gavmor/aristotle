# Sophia (Theoretical Wisdom)

Book VI, ch. 7 defines *sophia* as "knowledge and intellect of the things that are most honorable in their nature" — it "must be the most precise form of knowledge," combining [[concepts/nous|intellect's]] grasp of unprovable first principles with the demonstrative reasoning of scientific knowledge (*episteme*) built on top of them. Aristotle's example of the word's ordinary use is telling: we call Phidias a "wise" sculptor, meaning nothing more than that he is the most precise and accomplished at his art — but *sophia* in the unqualified sense belongs to someone "wise wholly, and not in part."

## Key Ideas

- **Sophia and [[concepts/phronesis|practical judgment]] are both intellectual virtues, but point in opposite directions.** Practical judgment is concerned with what is "good and advantageous for a human being" and with action, which is always about particular, changeable things; sophia is concerned with what is unchanging and universal — the most honorable objects of study, not the most useful. This is why Aristotle can raise a real impasse (ch. 12) about sophia's *use*: "wisdom contemplates nothing by which a human being will be happy, since it is not directed at any form of coming into being." ^[extracted]
- **Aristotle's provisional answer to that impasse**: wisdom "produces happiness... by being possessed and being at work," the way health produces health rather than the way medicine produces health — sophia doesn't make anything happen instrumentally, it simply *is*, in its own exercise, the activity that (at least in part) constitutes a good human life. This anticipates, without yet fully arguing, Book X's much stronger claim. ^[extracted]
- **Book X ch. 7-8 escalates sophia from "one of the virtues" to the candidate for complete happiness**: contemplation (*theoria*) — sophia's characteristic activity — is "the most complete, most continuous, most self-sufficient being-at-work available to a human being," and therefore [[concepts/contemplative-life|complete happiness]] itself, on the grounds that it is the activity of what is "most divine" in us and needs the fewest external conditions to be exercised. ^[extracted]
- **This creates the tension already flagged on [[concepts/eudaimonia]] and [[concepts/contemplative-life]]**: if sophia/contemplation is complete happiness, practical judgment and the virtues of character built around it (justice, courage, [[synthesis/crown-of-virtue|friendship]]) risk looking like second-tier, merely human happiness by comparison — a hierarchy Aristotle himself seems uneasy about, given how much of the rest of the book is devoted to the practical virtues. ^[inferred]

# Seeming: Phainesthai, Dokein, and Eoika

English "seem" and "appear" cover a lot of ground — visual impression, considered opinion, rough resemblance — that Greek keeps apart with separate verbs. Three consecutive sentences in Book I's honor passage (Bekker 1095b22-27) happen to use all three, back to back, which makes the passage a clean case study in how much a translation smooths over by reaching for one English word every time.

## Key Ideas

- **φαίνεται (*phainetai*) — appearing, coming to light.** Built on *phainō*, "to bring to light, show, reveal" — the same root as *phōs*, "light" (→ "phenomenon," "phase," "phantom"). When Aristotle says honor *phainetai* too superficial, the claim is about honor showing itself, becoming evident, on inspection — a manifestation-sense of "seeming," oriented toward what's visible once you look. ^[extracted]
- **δοκεῖ (*dokei*) — being opined, being judged to be.** From the root behind *doxa*, "opinion, reputation, belief" (→ "orthodox," "paradox," "doxology"). This is the very next verb in the same passage: honor *dokei* to reside more in the giver than the receiver. Where *phainetai* is about what shows itself, *dokei* is about what's *thought* — a matter of judgment or reputation, not direct manifestation. *Doxa* is also the term for the "received opinions" Aristotle's dialectical method regularly tests arguments against elsewhere in the work, which makes this verb's presence here worth flagging even beyond this one passage — an argument's *dokei* is exactly the kind of premise later chapters go on to interrogate. ^[inferred]
- **ἐοίκασι (*eoikasi*) — resembling, being like.** Perfect tense of *eoika*, "to be like, resemble" — the same root as *eikōn*, "image, likeness" (→ "icon," "iconic"). The very next sentence: people *eoikasi* pursuing honor in order to be convinced they're good. This is a comparison-based "seeming" — literally "they are like [people who do X for reason Y]" — the same verb form Aristotle reaches for constantly across the *Ethics* to introduce a simile or analogy rather than assert a plain fact. ^[inferred]
- **Three different epistemic postures, one English word.** Manifestation (*phainetai*), opinion (*dokei*), and resemblance (*eoikasi*) are not stylistic variation on a single idea — they're three distinct ways something can fail to be flatly asserted as fact, and Aristotle deploys the right one for each specific claim: honor *shows itself* superficial on inspection; it *is judged* to reside in the giver; people *resemble* those chasing self-confirmation. Flattening all three to "seems" in translation erases exactly this distinction. ^[inferred]
- **"To be in" is a plain locative, not a metaphor-marked one.** The clause *dokei... en tois timōsi... einai* ("[honor] is opined to be in the ones who give [it]") uses the ordinary preposition *en* ("in" — cognate with English "in") plus the bare infinitive "to be," identical to the grammar of "to be in the house." Aristotle's Greek describes honor's location using exactly the same construction as literal spatial containment, treating a social status as though it were a possession housed inside one party rather than a relation between two, or a quality residing in the recipient. ^[extracted]

*"But [honor] appears to be more superficial than what is being sought — for it is opined to lie more in those who bestow it than in the one honored, whereas we surmise that the good is something one's own and hard to take away."* Two seeming-verbs in one sentence: *phainetai* (honor shows itself superficial) and *dokei* (honor is opined to reside in the giver). See [[concepts/honor]] for the full argument this grounds.

### Bk. I, ch. 5 (Bekker 1095b25-27)

> ἔτι δʼ ἐοίκασι τὴν τιμὴν διώκειν ἵνα πιστεύσωσιν ἑαυτοὺς ἀγαθοὺς εἶναι.

```
ἔτι          δʼ   ἐοίκασι    τὴν      τιμὴν      διώκειν        ἵνα            πιστεύσωσιν            ἑαυτοὺς         ἀγαθοὺς      εἶναι
eti          d'   eoikasi    tēn      timēn      diōkein        hina           pisteusōsin            heautous        agathous     einai
furthermore  and  they-seem  the.ACC  honor.ACC  to-pursue.INF  in-order-that  they-may-be-convinced  themselves.ACC  good.ACC.PL  to-be.INF
```

*"Furthermore, they resemble [people who] pursue honor in order that they may be convinced that they themselves are good."* The third verb, *eoikasi* ("resemble"), diagnoses the psychology behind the behavior described by the first two: people chase something whose value even Aristotle has just located in the wrong party, because what they actually want is the self-confirming belief the chase produces — a resemblance-claim about their motive, not a report of what they see or a judgment about where honor lives.

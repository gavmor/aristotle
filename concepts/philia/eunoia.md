# Goodwill (Eunoia)

In Book IX, Chapter 5 of the *Nicomachean Ethics*, Aristotle defines **goodwill** (*eunoia*) as a necessary constituent component and origin point of friendship (*philia*), yet distinguishes it from active friendship itself.

## Key Ideas

- **Friendship Out-of-Work (*Argē Philia*):** Goodwill resembles friendship, but is not active friendship (*energeia*). Aristotle calls it "idle friendship" (*argēn philia*) — goodwill is friendship that has not yet become a being-at-work through extended familiarity and shared living (*suzēn*).
- **Distinction from Affection (*Philēsis*):** Goodwill does not involve emotional intensity or longing (*teinomenon*), which accompany active loving. It can arise instantaneously (e.g., spectator goodwill toward a contestant in a competition) without previous acquaintance.
- **Unidirectional vs. Reciprocal:** While true *philia* requires mutual awareness and reciprocal affection, goodwill can be felt toward someone who is unaware of it.
- **The Seed of Complete Friendship:** When goodwill is extended over time and reaches intimate familiarity (*synētheia*), it ripens into full friendship, provided it is based on virtue and the good rather than superficial utility or pleasure.

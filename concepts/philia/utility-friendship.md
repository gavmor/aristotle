# Friendship for Utility

In Book VIII, Chapters 2–3 of the *Nicomachean Ethics*, Aristotle defines **friendship for utility** as one of the two incidental (*kata symbebēkos*) species of friendship.

## Key Ideas

- **Incidental Basis:** Friends of this kind do not love the other person for who they are in themselves (*kath' hauton*), but only insofar as some material benefit or advantage (*chrēsimon*) is derived from them.
- **Easily Dissolved:** Because utility is not fixed and changes rapidly with circumstance, these friendships dissolve as soon as the mutual benefit ceases.
- **Demographic Prevalence:** Most common among older people (who seek help rather than pleasure) and adults engaged in business or trade relations. Host-guest relationships (*xenia*) often fall into this class.
- **Prone to Complaints:** Complaining (*egklēmata*) and disputes occur primarily in utility-based friendships, because each party measures the relationship by the gain received and constantly feels shortchanged.

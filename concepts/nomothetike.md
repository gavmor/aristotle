# Nomothetikē (Legislation and Political Science)

Book X, Chapter 9 of the *Nicomachean Ethics* forms the explicit bridge between Aristotle's ethical inquiry and his *Politics*, arguing that ethical knowledge is incomplete without the practical science of **legislation** (*nomothetikē*).

## Key Ideas

- **From Discourse to Practice:** In matters of human action (*praxis*), the ultimate goal is not merely knowing what virtue is, but *becoming* and *acting* virtuously. Discourses (*logoi*) can inspire noble and well-born youth, but fail to move the majority of people, who obey fear of penalties rather than shame or love of what is beautiful (*to kalon*). ^[extracted]
- **Preparing the Soil:** Just as ground must be cultivated before it can nourish seed, a person's soul must be prepared through habits (*ethē*) to enjoy and hate in a noble way before rational arguments can take root. Reason cannot alter long-standing passions; only force (*bia*) and habituation can. ^[extracted]
- **The Compulsory Power of Law:** Individual paternal commands lack force and are often hated when they oppose desires, whereas **law (*nomos*) possesses compulsory power (*anankastikē dynamis*)** while being speech derived from practical judgment (*phronēsis*) and intellect. Law is uniquely able to enforce moral discipline across a whole lifetime without provoking resentment. ^[extracted]
- **The Transition to the *Politics*:** Because very few cities (like Sparta) have public systems of moral upbringing, individuals must learn the legislative art (*nomothetikē*) to guide their own households. Aristotle concludes the *Ethics* by calling for a comprehensive study of constitutions, laws, and political decay/preservation—setting the stage directly for the *Politics*. ^[extracted]

# Feeling (Pathos)

In Book II, Chapter 5 of the [[references/nicomachean-ethics]], Aristotle identifies feelings (*pathē*, singular *pathos*) as one of the three kinds of things that come to be present in the [[concepts/soul|soul]], alongside [[concepts/predisposition|predispositions]] and [[concepts/hexis|active conditions]]. 

## Key Ideas

- **Definition**: Feelings are the passive experiences we undergo. Aristotle lists examples such as desire, anger, fear, confidence, envy, joy, affection, hatred, yearning, jealousy, and pity. In general, feelings are those things which are accompanied by pleasure or pain. ^[extracted]
- **Virtue is not a feeling**: Aristotle argues that virtue cannot be a feeling. We are not praised or blamed merely for feeling angry or afraid, but for *how* we feel them (e.g. violently, slackly, or in a measured way). Furthermore, feelings happen to us without deliberate choice, whereas virtues involve [[concepts/prohairesis|choice]]. ^[extracted]
- **Relation to active conditions**: While we are not judged by our feelings, our moral character (*hexis*) is determined by the settled way we bear ourselves toward these feelings. The virtuous person experiences feelings in the right amount, at the right time, toward the right objects, which is the core of the [[concepts/doctrine-of-the-mean]]. ^[extracted]
- **Lack of self-restraint**: In [[concepts/akrasia|akrasia]] (lack of self-restraint), feelings and impulses slip the reins of the reasoning part of the soul, acting as a passive, episodic condition rather than a stable active condition. ^[extracted]

# Cleverness (Deinotes)

In Book VI of the *Nicomachean Ethics*, Aristotle identifies **cleverness** (Greek: *deinotēs*) as the instrumental capacity "to enable one to do the things that are conducive to the object one has set down, and to achieve it." ^[extracted]

It is the raw, calculating power of the mind that works out *how* to get from point A to point B. Crucially, cleverness is entirely morally neutral regarding the destination itself.

## The Role of the Target

Because it only concerns the means, cleverness takes its moral color entirely from the end (the target) it serves: ^[extracted]
- If the target is noble/beautiful, cleverness is praiseworthy.
- If the target is base, cleverness manifests as **shamelessness** or **villainy** (*panourgia*). 

## Relationship to Practical Judgment (Phronesis)

Cleverness is the engine inside [[concepts/phronesis|practical judgment]], but it is not identical to it. 

Aristotle explains that practical judgment is not present *without* this capacity, but it is not *just* this capacity. Practical judgment only emerges when cleverness is governed by virtue of character, which ensures that the starting point (the end goal) is actually good. Without virtue, the capacity remains mere cleverness; vice warps the starting points of action, turning the clever person into a highly effective villain. ^[extracted]

As Aristotle notes, this is why people sometimes loosely call practically judicious people "clever and shameless" — they recognize the shared underlying capacity to get things done, even while missing the moral distinction that separates the two states. ^[extracted]

# Incapable of Being Otherwise (Necessity)

In Aristotle's framework, things that are **incapable of being otherwise** (Greek: μὴ ἐνδεχόμενα ἄλλως ἔχειν, *mē endechomena allōs echein*) constitute the realm of strict necessity. This ontological category stands in fundamental contrast to things that are [[concepts/capable-of-being-otherwise|capable of being otherwise]] (contingency).

## Key Characteristics

Things that exist by necessity share several absolute traits:
- They are **everlasting, ungenerated, and indestructible**. ^[extracted]
- Their nature is entirely fixed, meaning **no one deliberates about them**. ^[extracted]
- They constitute the proper objects of true knowledge (*episteme*) and demonstration, as their principles cannot be altered. ^[extracted]
- **The past** falls into this category: what has happened is not capable of not having happened, because its nature is now fixed in reality. ^[extracted]

Because these things are invariable, they are contemplated exclusively by the [[concepts/soul/knowing-part|knowing part]] of the rational soul.

Because things of absolute necessity are completely fixed, they are immune to human action; therefore, deliberation about them is structurally impossible.

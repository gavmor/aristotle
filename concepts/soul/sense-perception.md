# Sense-Perception (Aisthētikon)

Sense-perception is one of the three things in the soul (alongside intellect and desire) that govern action and truth (Book VI, Chapter 2).

## Key Ideas

- **No Origin of Action**: Aristotle argues that sense-perception itself originates no deliberate action. His proof is that animals possess sense-perception but do not share in action (meaning deliberate, rationally chosen conduct).
- **Role in Choice**: While it provides the necessary awareness of particulars, action is ultimately driven by the intersection of intellect and desire.

```ngloss
    τῇ [tē] [the.DAT]

\ft There are indeed three things in the soul that are the masters of action and truth: sense-perception, intellect, and desire.
```

In this passage, Aristotle sets up the psychological triad that governs human interaction with the world. He immediately clarifies in the following line that *aisthēsis* (sense-perception) originates no action, thereby isolating intellect and desire as the true engines of moral choice.

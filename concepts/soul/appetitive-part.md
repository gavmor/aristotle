# Appetitive Part (Epithumētikon)

The appetitive or desiring part of the soul is a subdivision of the [[concepts/soul/irrational-part|irrational soul]] that nevertheless participates in reason (Book I, Chapter 13).

## Key Ideas

- **Obedience to Reason**: While it does not generate rational thought itself, it shares in reason by its capacity to listen to and obey it, analogous to how one listens to a parent. 
- **Seat of Moral Virtue**: Because this part can be habituated into a stable [[concepts/hexis|active condition]] that aligns perfectly with rational judgment, it is the locus of all the virtues of character (like courage and temperance).
- **Conflict**: In states like [[concepts/akrasia|incontinence]], this part rebels against the rational part's judgments.

Here in Book VII, Aristotle vividly describes the psychological mechanics of incontinence (akrasia). The *epithumia* (appetite/desire) acts as a distinct motivational vector that physically pulls the agent toward the pleasant object, overpowering the rational judgment that counsels restraint.

# Rational Part (Logon echon)

The rational part of the [[concepts/soul/soul|soul]] (*logon echon*) is the half of Aristotle's fundamental bipartite moral psychology that possesses reason inherently and directly.

## Key Ideas

- **Division by Objects**: In Book VI, Chapter 1, Aristotle subdivides the rational part into two distinct faculties based on the nature of the objects they contemplate:
  1. The **[[concepts/soul/knowing-part|Knowing Part]]** (*epistēmonikon*), which grasps invariable truths (things [[concepts/incapable-of-being-otherwise|incapable of being otherwise]]).
  2. The **[[concepts/soul/calculating-part|Calculating Part]]** (*logistikon*), which deliberates about variable truths (things [[concepts/capable-of-being-otherwise|capable of being otherwise]]).
- **Virtues of Thought**: This part of the soul is the seat of the intellectual virtues (the five truth-disclosing powers): intellect, knowledge, wisdom, practical judgment, and art.

Here, Aristotle formally recaps his bipartite division from Book I before further subdividing the rational part. Note that he uses *logon echon* (literally "having reason") rather than a single adjective, emphasizing that this part *possesses* reason inherently.

### Bk. VI, ch. 1 (Bekker 1139a6-8)

# Eleutheriotes

**Deficiency:** Stinginess (Aneleutheria)
**Excess:** Wastefulness (Asōtia)

**Eleutheriotes** (often translated as Generosity or Liberality) is a moral virtue discussed by Aristotle in Book IV, Chapter 1 of the *Nicomachean Ethics*. It is the mean condition concerning the giving and taking of wealth or money, characterized primarily by the proper and beautiful use of one's resources.

## Summary
Eleutheriotes relates to material wealth—specifically, anything whose value is measured by money. While it involves both giving and getting, generosity is fundamentally demonstrated through the *giving* of wealth. For Aristotle, it is more characteristic of virtue to do good (giving) than to be treated well (receiving), and to do beautiful actions rather than simply avoid shameful ones. Thus, the generous person gives the right amounts to the right people at the right time, and does so with pleasure (or at least without pain) for the sake of the beautiful (*to kalon*).

## Key Ideas
* **Proportionate Giving:** Generosity is not determined by the absolute amount given but by the "active condition of the giver" relative to their means. A person with less money can be more generous than a wealthy person if they give a larger proportion of their wealth.
* **The Motive of the Beautiful:** The generous person gives "for the sake of the beautiful" (*to kalon*). They are not merely thoughtless with money, but thoughtfully bestow it where it is right, beautiful, and fitting. 
* **Proper Taking and Preserving:** While focusing on giving, the generous person must also take money from proper sources (e.g., their own possessions) and preserve it so they have the means to give to the right people. They do not take from wrong sources, nor do they hold wealth in too high esteem.
* **Attitude toward Wealth:** The generous person cares little for money itself; they value it instrumentally, purely for the sake of giving it away to assist others. As a result, generous people are highly loved, but they often struggle to become or stay wealthy.

## Extremes (Vices)
Like all moral virtues, Eleutheriotes is a mean between two vices:
* **Excess: Wastefulness (*Asotia*)** 
  Going to excess in giving and falling short in taking. The wasteful person is somewhat self-destructive by ruining their own means of living. However, Aristotle considers this vice less base and more curable than stinginess (often cured by age or lack of resources), as the wasteful person at least benefits others. Uncorrected, wastefulness often devolves into taking from wrong sources to fund unchecked spending, leading to dissipation.
* **Deficiency: Stinginess (*Aneleutheria*)** 
  Falling short in giving and (sometimes) going to excess in taking. Stinginess is seen as a worse and more incurable vice, deeply ingrained in human nature, as most people are "lovers of money." It manifests in various forms, from the tight-fisted miser who neither gives nor takes out of a cautious self-preservation, to those who engage in shameful trades (like pimps, thieves, or loan sharks) to take money from improper sources.

## Relationships
* **Magnificence (*Megaloprepeia*):** Both virtues involve the proper use of wealth, but while Eleutheriotes covers all everyday monetary matters, Magnificence specifically concerns large-scale, lavish, and public expenditures. A magnificent person is necessarily generous, but a generous person is not necessarily magnificent (due to lack of vast wealth).
* **The Beautiful (*To Kalon*):** The primary motivation for the generous person's actions. Giving must be directed toward what is beautiful.
* **Justice:** Generosity goes beyond justice. A person who simply doesn't take what belongs to others is praised for justice, but true generosity requires the active, voluntary giving of one's own resources.

# Self-Love (Philautia)

Book IX, ch. 8 takes up a puzzle generated by [[concepts/philia|the friendship books]] themselves: all the marks of a friend — wishing someone well for their own sake, spending time with them, sharing their joys and pains — "arise out of oneself and extend to others," so that "one is a friend to oneself most of all." Yet "self-lover" is an ordinary term of reproach. Aristotle resolves the tension by arguing the word is used in two genuinely different senses, not one.

## Key Ideas

- **The reproached sense**: someone who "distribute[s] a greater amount to himself in the way of money, honors, and bodily pleasures" — gratifying "the irrational part of the soul." Aristotle grants this is the *majority* use of the word, and that it deserves the reproach it gets, "since most people are of this kind." ^[extracted]
- **The good sense, discovered by asking what "oneself" really refers to**: just as a city is most truly identified with its most authoritative part, a human being is most truly identified with intellect — "the things people seem most of all to have done themselves and willingly are the things they have done with reason." Someone who "gratifies what is most authoritative in himself" — who takes for himself the most beautiful and good actions, at whatever cost to money, honor, or comfort — is a self-lover in a wholly different, praiseworthy sense: "differing as much as living by reason does from living by passion." ^[extracted]
- **A decent person's self-love and other-directedness turn out not to compete.** Because what he loves most is his own capacity to act beautifully, a good person will give away money, honors, and even actions themselves to friends — "it would be a more beautiful thing to become responsible for the friend's performing them than to perform them himself" — since securing [[concepts/to-kalon|the beautiful]] for himself is what his self-love actually consists in, and beautiful action toward friends counts as much as beautiful action anywhere else. He therefore "distributes to himself the greater share of the beautiful" precisely by appearing to sacrifice the ordinary goods (money, honor, comfort) that the reproached self-lover hoards. ^[extracted]
- **This directly resolves rather than sidesteps the reproach**: "a good person ought to be a lover of self, since he will both profit himself and benefit others... a corrupt person ought not, since he will harm himself and those around him." Self-love isn't rejected as a category; it's split into a form that damages the community and a form that is, in the end, identical with virtue itself. ^[extracted]

## Greek Gloss

Source: Bk. IX, ch. 8 (Bekker 1168b19–22).

> χαρίζονται ταῖς ἐπιθυμίαις καὶ ὅλως τοῖς πάθεσι καὶ τῷ ἀλόγῳ τῆς ψυχῆς· τοιοῦτοι δʼ εἰσὶν οἱ πολλοί· διὸ καὶ ἡ προσηγορία γεγένηται ἀπὸ τοῦ πολλοῦ φαύλου ὄντος· δικαίως δὴ τοῖς οὕτω φιλαύτοις ὀνειδίζεται.

| ἀ- | λόγ- | -ον |
|---|---|---|
| *a-* | *log-* | *-on* |
| privative prefix, "without," "not" | root of *logos*, "reason," "reckoning," "speech" | adjectival ending |
| → **ἄλογον**, "irrational," "without reason" | | |

The reproached self-lover is defined by exactly this word — gratifying the part of the soul that has no share in *logos* is what makes the ordinary sense of self-love a term of blame.

Source: Bk. IX, ch. 8 (Bekker 1168b30–33).

> ὥσπερ δὲ καὶ πόλις τὸ κυριώτατον μάλιστʼ εἶναι δοκεῖ καὶ πᾶν ἄλλο σύστημα, οὕτω καὶ ἄνθρωπος· καὶ φίλαυτος δὴ μάλιστα ὁ τοῦτο ἀγαπῶν καὶ τούτῳ χαριζόμενος.

| κυρι- | -ώ- | -τατον |
|---|---|---|
| *kyri-* | *-ō-* | *-taton* |
| root of *kyrios*, "authoritative," "sovereign," "in control" | connecting vowel | superlative suffix, "-est" |
| → **κυριώτατον**, "most sovereign," "most authoritative" | | |

Aristotle's city/human analogy locates the true self in whichever part rules, not in appetite — which is the whole basis for saying the good sense of self-love loves intellect rather than the body.

Source: Bk. IX, ch. 8 (Bekker 1169a3–6).

> διὸ φίλαυτος μάλιστʼ ἂν εἴη, καθʼ ἕτερον εἶδος τοῦ ὀνειδιζομένου, καὶ διαφέρων τοσοῦτον ὅσον τὸ κατὰ λόγον ζῆν τοῦ κατὰ πάθος, καὶ ὀρέγεσθαι ἢ τοῦ καλοῦ ἢ τοῦ δοκοῦντος συμφέρειν.

| λόγ- | -ος |
|---|---|
| *log-* | *-os* |
| root meaning "to gather, reckon, give an account" — the capacity for reasoned speech | noun-forming suffix |
| → **λόγος**, "reason," "a rational account" | |

The entire distance between the two senses of self-love is compressed into this opposition of *logos* to *pathos* — reasoned life against life driven by feeling.

Source: Bk. IX, ch. 8 (Bekker 1169a32–34).

> ἐνδέχεται δὲ καὶ πράξεις τῷ φίλῳ προΐεσθαι, καὶ εἶναι κάλλιον τοῦ αὐτὸν πρᾶξαι τὸ αἴτιον τῷ φίλῳ γενέσθαι.

| καλλ- | -ῑον |
|---|---|
| *kall-* | *-īon* |
| root of *kalos*, "beautiful," "noble," "fine" (stem doubles in the comparative) | comparative suffix, "more" |
| → **κάλλιον**, "more beautiful," "nobler" | |

The comparative of *kalos* carries the whole point: ceding a beautiful action to a friend is judged more beautiful than doing it oneself, so generosity and self-love turn out to want the same thing.

Source: Bk. IX, ch. 8 (Bekker 1169a11–15).

> ὥστε τὸν μὲν ἀγαθὸν δεῖ φίλαυτον εἶναι (καὶ γὰρ αὐτὸς ὀνήσεται τὰ καλὰ πράττων καὶ τοὺς ἄλλους ὠφελήσει), τὸν δὲ μοχθηρὸν οὐ δεῖ· βλάψει γὰρ καὶ ἑαυτὸν καὶ τοὺς πέλας, φαύλοις πάθεσιν ἑπόμενος.

| φίλ- | αὐτ- | -ος |
|---|---|---|
| *phil-* | *aut-* | *-os* |
| root of *philos*, "dear," "loving," "friend" | root of *autos*, "self," "same" | adjectival ending |
| → **φίλαυτος**, "self-loving," "a lover of one's own self" | | |

This is the very word the chapter has been arguing over: Aristotle's verdict that the good person must be one and the base person must not shows the term dividing along the line of virtue rather than vanishing.

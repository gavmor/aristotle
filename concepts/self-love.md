# Self-Love (Philautia)

Book IX, ch. 8 takes up a puzzle generated by [[concepts/philia|the friendship books]] themselves: all the marks of a friend — wishing someone well for their own sake, spending time with them, sharing their joys and pains — "arise out of oneself and extend to others," so that "one is a friend to oneself most of all." Yet "self-lover" is an ordinary term of reproach. Aristotle resolves the tension by arguing the word is used in two genuinely different senses, not one.

## Key Ideas

- **The reproached sense**: someone who "distribute[s] a greater amount to himself in the way of money, honors, and bodily pleasures" — gratifying "the irrational part of the soul." Aristotle grants this is the *majority* use of the word, and that it deserves the reproach it gets, "since most people are of this kind." ^[extracted]
- **The good sense, discovered by asking what "oneself" really refers to**: just as a city is most truly identified with its most authoritative part, a human being is most truly identified with intellect — "the things people seem most of all to have done themselves and willingly are the things they have done with reason." Someone who "gratifies what is most authoritative in himself" — who takes for himself the most beautiful and good actions, at whatever cost to money, honor, or comfort — is a self-lover in a wholly different, praiseworthy sense: "differing as much as living by reason does from living by passion." ^[extracted]
- **A decent person's self-love and other-directedness turn out not to compete.** Because what he loves most is his own capacity to act beautifully, a good person will give away money, honors, and even actions themselves to friends — "it would be a more beautiful thing to become responsible for the friend's performing them than to perform them himself" — since securing [[concepts/to-kalon|the beautiful]] for himself is what his self-love actually consists in, and beautiful action toward friends counts as much as beautiful action anywhere else. He therefore "distributes to himself the greater share of the beautiful" precisely by appearing to sacrifice the ordinary goods (money, honor, comfort) that the reproached self-lover hoards. ^[extracted]
- **This directly resolves rather than sidesteps the reproach**: "a good person ought to be a lover of self, since he will both profit himself and benefit others... a corrupt person ought not, since he will harm himself and those around him." Self-love isn't rejected as a category; it's split into a form that damages the community and a form that is, in the end, identical with virtue itself. ^[extracted]

*They gratify their desires and, in general, their feelings and the irrational part of the soul; such people make up the majority, which is why the name arose from the majority being base — so those called self-lovers in this way are justly reproached.* This is the reproached sense the page's first bullet describes: the target of the reproach is fixed by *ἀλόγῳ* (dative of *alogon* — the privative prefix ἀ- "not" fused to the *λόγ-* root of *logos*, "reason," plus an adjectival ending), the part of the soul that simply has no share in reasoned speech at all.

*Just as a city seems most of all to be its most authoritative element, and so does every other composite whole, so too a human being — and it is the one who loves this element and gratifies it who is a self-lover most of all.* The whole city/human analogy that grounds the good sense of self-love turns on *κυριώτατον* (κυρι-, root of *kyrios*, "authoritative, sovereign," plus a connecting vowel and the superlative suffix -τατον, "most"): the true self is whatever part actually rules, not whichever part shouts loudest.

*It is also possible to give up actions to a friend, and it is more beautiful to become the cause of the friend's performing them than to do them oneself.* The comparative *κάλλιον* (καλλ-, the doubled stem of *kalos*, "beautiful, noble," plus the comparative suffix -ῑον, "more") carries the whole argument of the page's third bullet: ceding the beautiful act to a friend outranks performing it oneself, so a good person's self-love and generosity turn out to want the identical thing.

# Continence / Self-Restraint (Enkrateia)

Book VII names *enkrateia* — self-restraint — as the positive counterpart to [[concepts/akrasia|unrestraint]], one of the three things named opposite the three things to be avoided (vice, unrestraint, brutishness) at the book's opening. The self-restrained person is "the same as one who stands by his reasoning": he has strong, base desires, exactly as the unrestrained person does, but does not act on them.

## Key Ideas

- **The genuinely puzzling case, not the obvious one.** Aristotle raises a real impasse in ch. 2: if self-restraint requires *having* strong, base desires and successfully not following them, is it actually praiseworthy? If the desires were good, resisting them would be bad; if weak, resisting them is no great feat. The condition only counts as admirable because the desires are both base *and* strong — self-restraint is a real victory over a real internal opponent, not the absence of a fight. ^[extracted]
- **Distinguished sharply from temperance, which akrasia.md already establishes**: the self-restrained person has a struggle to win; the temperate person has no base desires of that strength to begin with. Aristotle explicitly ranks temperance higher for this reason — the fight itself is evidence of an imperfectly ordered soul, even when reason wins it. ^[extracted]
- **Self-restraint (about pleasure) is distinguished from endurance (about pain), and ranked above it.** Its opposite, softness, is opposed by *endurance* (*karteria*), not by self-restraint itself: "the enduring person is the opposite of someone who is soft... for enduring consists in holding out against something, but self-restraint consists in overpowering it, and holding out and overpowering are different, just as not giving way is different from being victorious. Hence self-restraint is preferable to endurance." Self-restraint defeats the desire; endurance merely doesn't yield to the pain. ^[extracted]
- **Not every failure to stand firm is unrestraint, and not every case of standing firm is culpable stubbornness** — Aristotle's recurring test case is Neoptolemus in Sophocles's *Philoctetes*, who breaks his promise to Odysseus (to keep lying) because he's pained at lying, and is praised for it: giving way to *a beautiful pleasure* (truthfulness) is not the same failure as giving way to a shameful one. Standing firm on a false opinion, likewise, is not automatically a virtue — self-restraint is only praiseworthy when what one stands firm on is actually right. ^[extracted]
- **Gradations of forgivability track the strength of what's resisted**: giving way to pleasures or pains that are "strong and excessive" is more forgivable than giving way to ordinary ones "that most people are able to hold out against" — someone undone by an ordinary temptation reveals more about his character than someone overwhelmed by a genuinely extreme one. ^[extracted]

## Greek Gloss

**The opening puzzle: is self-restraint even praiseworthy?**

Source: Bk. VII, ch. 2 (Bekker 1146a).

> εἰ μὲν γὰρ χρησταὶ αἱ ἐπιθυμίαι, φαύλη ἡ κωλύουσα ἕξις μὴ ἀκολουθεῖν, ὥσθʼ ἡ ἐγκράτεια οὐ πᾶσα σπουδαία· εἰ δʼ ἀσθενεῖς καὶ μὴ φαῦλαι, οὐθὲν σεμνόν, οὐδʼ εἰ φαῦλαι καὶ ἀσθενεῖς, οὐδὲν μέγα.

| σπουδ- | -αία |
|---|---|
| *spoud-* | *-aia* |
| root of σπουδή, "haste, zeal, earnest effort" | adjectival suffix, "characterized by" |
| → **σπουδαία**, "earnest," "of serious worth," "praiseworthy" | |

The whole puzzle turns on this one word: self-restraint only earns the label σπουδαῖος if the desires overpowered are genuinely bad *and* genuinely strong — Aristotle is checking the concept against both boundary conditions before he'll let it stand as a virtue-adjacent state.

**Neoptolemus: a beautiful failure to stand firm**

Source: Bk. VII, ch. 2 (Bekker 1146a).

> ἔσται τις σπουδαία ἀκρασία, οἷον ὁ Σοφοκλέους Νεοπτόλεμος ἐν τῷ Φιλοκτήτῃ· ἐπαινετὸς γὰρ οὐκ ἐμμένων οἷς ἐπείσθη ὑπὸ τοῦ Ὀδυσσέως διὰ τὸ λυπεῖσθαι ψευδόμενος.

| ἐπ(ι)- | αἰν- | -ετός |
|---|---|---|
| *ep(i)-* | *ain-* | *-etos* |
| "upon, toward" | root of αἰνέω, "to tell of, to praise" | verbal-adjective suffix, "-able," "-worthy" |
| → **ἐπαινετός**, "praiseworthy," "commendable" | | |

Aristotle calls Neoptolemus ἐπαινετός precisely *for* abandoning his agreement with Odysseus — the same word normally reserved for holding firm is here awarded for giving way, because what he gave way to was truthfulness rather than a shameful pleasure.

**Self-restraint outranks endurance**

Source: Bk. VII, ch. 7 (Bekker 1150a).

> ἀντίκειται δὲ τῷ μὲν ἀκρατεῖ ὁ ἐγκρατής, τῷ δὲ μαλακῷ ὁ καρτερικός· τὸ μὲν γὰρ καρτερεῖν ἐστὶν ἐν τῷ ἀντέχειν, ἡ δʼ ἐγκράτεια ἐν τῷ κρατεῖν, ἕτερον δὲ τὸ ἀντέχειν καὶ κρατεῖν, ὥσπερ καὶ τὸ μὴ ἡττᾶσθαι τοῦ νικᾶν· διὸ καὶ αἱρετώτερον ἐγκράτεια καρτερίας ἐστίν.

| ἐν- | κρατ- | -εια |
|---|---|---|
| *en-* | *krat-* | *-eia* |
| "in" | root of κράτος, "power, mastery, strength" | abstract-noun suffix |
| → **ἐγκράτεια**, "a being-in-mastery," "self-mastery" | | |

Aristotle's own etymological move: καρτερεῖν is only *ἀντέχειν* ("holding out against"), but ἐγκράτεια is *κρατεῖν* ("mastering") — the preposition ἐν plus κράτος makes self-restraint an active overpowering rather than a passive not-yielding, which is exactly why he ranks it above endurance.

**Gradations of forgivability**

Source: Bk. VII, ch. 7 (Bekker 1150b).

> οὐ γὰρ εἴ τις ἰσχυρῶν καὶ ὑπερβαλλουσῶν ἡδονῶν ἡττᾶται ἢ λυπῶν, θαυμαστόν, ἀλλὰ συγγνωμονικὸν εἰ ἀντιτείνων […] ἀλλʼ εἴ τις πρὸς ἃς οἱ πολλοὶ δύνανται ἀντέχειν, τούτων ἡττᾶται καὶ μὴ δύναται ἀντιτείνειν, μὴ διὰ φύσιν τοῦ γένους ἢ διὰ νόσον.

| συν- | γνωμ- | -ονικόν |
|---|---|---|
| *syn-* | *gnōm-* | *-onikon* |
| "with, together" | root of γνώμη / γιγνώσκειν, "judgment, knowing" | adjectival suffix |
| → **συγγνωμονικός**, "sharing judgment with," "forgivable," "excusable" | | |

συγγνώμη is literally a "knowing-together" — the forgivability Aristotle grants scales with how extreme the pleasure or pain was, so that being overcome by something most people can resist earns none of this shared understanding, while losing to something genuinely extreme does.

**Continence only resembles temperance**

Source: Bk. VII, ch. 9 (Bekker 1151b–1152a).

> ἐπεὶ δὲ καθʼ ὁμοιότητα πολλὰ λέγεται, καὶ ἡ ἐγκράτεια ἡ τοῦ σώφρονος καθʼ ὁμοιότητα ἠκολούθηκεν· ὅ τε γὰρ ἐγκρατὴς οἷος μηδὲν παρὰ τὸν λόγον διὰ τὰς σωματικὰς ἡδονὰς ποιεῖν καὶ ὁ σώφρων, ἀλλʼ ὃ μὲν ἔχων ὃ δʼ οὐκ ἔχων φαύλας ἐπιθυμίας.

| ὁμοι- | -ό- | -τητα |
|---|---|---|
| *homoi-* | (linking vowel) | *-tēta* |
| root of ὅμοιος, "like, similar" | | abstract-noun suffix, "-ness" (acc. sg.) |
| → **ὁμοιότης**, "likeness," "resemblance" | | |

Aristotle flags his own comparison as only καθʼ ὁμοιότητα, "by resemblance" — the self-restrained and the temperate person act alike toward bodily pleasure, but one of them still has the base desires to fight off and the other simply doesn't, which is the whole basis for ranking temperance higher.

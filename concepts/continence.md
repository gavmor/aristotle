# Continence / Self-Restraint (Enkrateia)

Book VII names *enkrateia* — self-restraint — as the positive counterpart to [[concepts/akrasia|unrestraint]], one of the three things named opposite the three things to be avoided (vice, unrestraint, brutishness) at the book's opening. The self-restrained person is "the same as one who stands by his reasoning": he has strong, base desires, exactly as the unrestrained person does, but does not act on them.

## Key Ideas

- **The genuinely puzzling case, not the obvious one.** Aristotle raises a real impasse in ch. 2: if self-restraint requires *having* strong, base desires and successfully not following them, is it actually praiseworthy? If the desires were good, resisting them would be bad; if weak, resisting them is no great feat. The condition only counts as admirable because the desires are both base *and* strong — self-restraint is a real victory over a real internal opponent, not the absence of a fight. ^[extracted]
- **Distinguished sharply from temperance, which akrasia.md already establishes**: the self-restrained person has a struggle to win; the temperate person has no base desires of that strength to begin with. Aristotle explicitly ranks temperance higher for this reason — the fight itself is evidence of an imperfectly ordered soul, even when reason wins it. ^[extracted]
- **Self-restraint (about pleasure) is distinguished from endurance (about pain), and ranked above it.** Its opposite, softness, is opposed by *endurance* (*karteria*), not by self-restraint itself: "the enduring person is the opposite of someone who is soft... for enduring consists in holding out against something, but self-restraint consists in overpowering it, and holding out and overpowering are different, just as not giving way is different from being victorious. Hence self-restraint is preferable to endurance." Self-restraint defeats the desire; endurance merely doesn't yield to the pain. ^[extracted]
- **Not every failure to stand firm is unrestraint, and not every case of standing firm is culpable stubbornness** — Aristotle's recurring test case is Neoptolemus in Sophocles's *Philoctetes*, who breaks his promise to Odysseus (to keep lying) because he's pained at lying, and is praised for it: giving way to *a beautiful pleasure* (truthfulness) is not the same failure as giving way to a shameful one. Standing firm on a false opinion, likewise, is not automatically a virtue — self-restraint is only praiseworthy when what one stands firm on is actually right. ^[extracted]
- **Gradations of forgivability track the strength of what's resisted**: giving way to pleasures or pains that are "strong and excessive" is more forgivable than giving way to ordinary ones "that most people are able to hold out against" — someone undone by an ordinary temptation reveals more about his character than someone overwhelmed by a genuinely extreme one. ^[extracted]

*"For if the desires are good, then the condition that prevents following them is bad, so self-restraint would not always be admirable; but if the desires are weak and not bad, there is nothing solemn about resisting them, and if they are bad and weak, it is no great feat either."* This is the impasse the page's first bullet raises: the label σπουδαία ("praiseworthy," built on the σπουδή-root for earnest, zealous effort plus the adjectival -αία ending) only attaches to self-restraint when the desires overpowered are checked against both boundary conditions — bad *and* strong — at once.

*"The self-restrained person is opposed to the unrestrained, and the enduring person to the soft one; for enduring consists in holding out, while self-restraint consists in mastering, and holding out and mastering are different things, just as not being defeated differs from conquering — which is why self-restraint is more choiceworthy than endurance."* Aristotle builds his own etymological argument into the sentence: καρτερεῖν is glossed as mere ἀντέχειν ("holding out against"), but ἐγκράτεια is glossed as κρατεῖν ("mastering") — the preposition ἐν ("in") fused onto the κρατ- root of κράτος ("power, mastery") makes self-restraint an active overpowering rather than a passive not-yielding, which is exactly why it outranks endurance.

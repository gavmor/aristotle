# Athanatizein (Immortalizing / Striving for Immortality)

In Book X, Chapter 7 of the *Nicomachean Ethics* (Bekker 1177b33), Aristotle issues one of his most celebrated and surprising injunctions: **`ἐφʼ ὅσον ἐνδέχεται ἀθανατίζειν`** (*ep' hoson endechetai athanatizein* — "to immortalize oneself as far as possible").

## Key Ideas

- **Rejection of Conventional Mortal Humility:** Aristotle explicitly rejects the traditional Greek proverbial advice (voiced by poets like Pindar and Sophocles) that humans should "think human thoughts, being human, and mortal thoughts, being mortal." ^[extracted]
- **The Divine Element in Man:** While human beings are compound creatures (*syntheton*) bound to bodily passions and social necessities, the intellect (*nous*) is something divine (*theion*) indwelling within us. Insofar as a person lives by intellect, they live not merely a human life, but a divine one. ^[extracted]
- **Power and Worth over Bulk:** Even though the intellect occupies a small part of a human being in physical bulk, in power (*dynamis*) and worth (*timē*) it vastly surpasses all the rest of our compound nature. Indeed, Aristotle claims that each person *is* this governing part most of all. ^[extracted]
- **The Highest Happiness:** Because intellect is the most authoritative and divine element in human nature, the activity of contemplation (*theōria*) is the supreme fulfillment of human *eudaimonia*. To live in accord with intellect is to achieve the divine likeness accessible to mortals. ^[extracted]

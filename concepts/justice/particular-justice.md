# Particular Justice

## Summary
In Book V, Chapter 2 of the *Nicomachean Ethics*, Aristotle distinguishes "particular justice" (or justice as a part of virtue) from "complete justice" (or universal justice, which is synonymous with lawfulness and complete virtue in relation to others). Particular justice is concerned specifically with fairness, equality, and the avoidance of grasping for more than one's fair share. It deals directly with the equitable division of divisible goods—such as honor, money, and safety—and with rectifying imbalances in interpersonal transactions.

## Key Ideas
- **Distinct from Complete Virtue:** While complete justice encompasses all virtuous actions directed towards others (e.g., being brave or temperate for the sake of the community), particular justice is a specific, distinct virtue. It stands alongside other moral virtues like courage and temperance, and is specifically opposed to the vice of grasping or taking more than one's fair share of goods (or less of bad things).
- **The Motive of Gain:** Aristotle highlights the motive of the action to clarify this distinction. For example, someone who commits adultery purely out of lust acts from the vice of dissipation (a lack of self-restraint). However, if someone commits adultery for the sake of financial profit, they are acting out of the specific vice of particular injustice—driven by the pleasure of unfair gain rather than bodily desire.
- **The Realm of Divisible Goods:** Particular justice focuses on matters where individuals can have equal or unequal shares. Its domain is whatever a "serious person is serious about" when it comes to material or social goods, where one person's gain inherently means another person's loss.
- **Two Forms of Particular Justice:** At the end of Chapter 2, Aristotle outlines the two main branches of particular justice:
  1. **Distributive Justice:** Concerned with the fair allocation of divisible goods (like wealth or honors) among members of a political community. This type of justice operates on proportional equality, meaning goods are distributed according to an individual's merit or contribution.
  2. **Corrective (or Rectificatory) Justice:** Concerned with "setting things straight" in transactions between individuals. It focuses on arithmetic equality, aiming to rectify unfair gains or losses to restore the original balance. This applies to both willing transactions (like buying, selling, or lending) and unwilling ones (such as theft, assault, or fraud).

Here Aristotle draws the crucial distinction between committing an unjust act and being an unjust person. The presence or absence of *[[concepts/prohairesis|prohairesis]]* (deliberate choice) determines character. The man driven by *[[feeling|pathos]]* (passion) does commit an injustice, but because the act does not originate in deliberate choice, he is not classed as an "adulterer" by character. This reinforces the broader point about motive separating particular injustice (driven by grasping for gain) from other vices or momentary passions.


## Relationships
- **[[distributive-justice|Distributive Justice]]:** One of the two primary sub-categories of particular justice, focusing on proportional allocation.
- **[[corrective-justice|Corrective Justice]]:** The second primary sub-category of particular justice, focusing on restoring arithmetic equality in transactions.
- **[[universal-justice|Universal Justice]]:** The broader category from which particular justice is differentiated. While particular justice deals strictly with fair shares and equity, universal justice encompasses all lawful and virtuous behavior directed toward others.

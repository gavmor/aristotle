# Decency (Epieikeia)

In Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* (Book V), **Epieikeia** is the virtue of decency or equity. It is deeply related to justice, serving as a corrective to legally codified justice. Because laws must be written as universal rules, they cannot account for every unique, particular situation. The decent or equitable person (*epieikēs*) is one who recognizes when the rigid application of the law would actually produce an unjust result, and voluntarily takes less than their strict legal rights in order to preserve the *spirit* of justice.

Bk. V, ch. 10 takes up *epieikeia* — "usually translated in this chapter as 'equity' in its technical legal sense," a footnote in this edition notes, but "this is misleading." Translator Joe Sachs renders it "decency" instead, joining [[concepts/hexis|hexis]], [[concepts/energeia|energeia]], and [[concepts/to-kalon|to kalon]] as another deliberate departure from the standard English tradition: the glossary entry is explicit that Aristotle "is describing something that goes beyond what is equitable (*to ison*)" — decency isn't a technical legal-equity doctrine but one of "Aristotle's most frequent ways of naming human goodness."

## Key Ideas

- **The puzzle decency resolves**: is what's decent the same as what's just, or different? Aristotle says both intuitions are partly right — "the decent thing, though it is better than a certain kind of just thing, is just, and is not better than what is just by being of some other kind." Decency isn't a rival standard to justice; it's a correction *internal* to justice, applied when strict legal justice, precisely because it's stated universally, produces the wrong answer in a particular case.
- **The reason lies in what a law necessarily is**: "every law is universal, and there are some things about which it is not possible to speak rightly when speaking universally." A law "takes in what applies to the greater number of cases, not failing to recognize the error" — the lawmaker isn't at fault, and neither is the law; "the error is not in the law or in the lawmaker, but in the nature of a particular case," since "the material of which actions consist is that way straight from the start."
- **The Lesbian ruler**: "a rule for something indeterminate is also indeterminate, as is the lead straightedge used in housebuilding on Lesbos; for the ruler is altered to fit the shape of the stone and does not stay rigid." Decency is this ruler applied to law: not abandoning the law's aim, but bending the letter of it back to what "the lawmaker himself would say if he were there," had he foreseen the case.
- **A decent person is defined by a specific, almost counterintuitive habit**: someone "inclined to choose and to do things of that sort," who is "not rigidly precise about justice to a fault, but is inclined to take less, even when he has the support of the law." Decency, in other words, shows up as a disposition to *forgo* a strict legal entitlement, not merely as clever legal interpretation.
- **This sits one level above [[concepts/corrective-justice|the courtroom machinery]] already documented elsewhere**: where corrective justice supplies the mechanism for setting transactions right by an equal, arithmetic mean, decency supplies the standard for when the *letter* of that mechanism should yield to what the case actually calls for — the same "setting straight" language Aristotle uses for corrective justice itself (*diorthoun*) recurs here: decency is called "a setting straight of what is legally just."

Aristotle defines decency (*epieikeia*) not as a virtue entirely distinct from justice, but as a specific, superior *kind* of justice that corrects the rigid universality of written law. The decent person does not strictly stand on their legal rights when doing so would cause harm (*akribodikaios epi to cheiron*); this active condition of taking less than one's legal due (*elattōtikos*) is, ultimately, what true justice looks like in complex human situations.

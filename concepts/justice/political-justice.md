# Political Justice vs Paternal/Master Justice

In Book V, Chapter 6 of Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics*, a critical distinction is made between **Political Justice** (justice in the unqualified sense) and **Paternal/Master Justice** (justice only by likeness or analogy).

## Summary
Aristotle argues that true, unqualified justice—**Political Justice**—can only exist among free and relatively equal citizens who share a life aimed at self-sufficiency and are subject to the rule of law. In contrast, the relationship between a master and a slave, or a father and a young child, is governed by **Master/Paternal Justice**. This secondary form of justice exists because slaves and dependents are viewed as extensions or "parts" of the master/father, and since one cannot commit an injustice against oneself, true political justice does not apply.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Conditions of Political Justice
For political justice to exist, several preconditions must be met:
- **Freedom and Equality:** Individuals must be free and equal, either numerically (absolute equality) or proportionally (based on merit).
- **Rule of Law:** There must be a system of law that regulates their interactions, meaning there is an "equality of ruling and being ruled."
- **Self-Sufficiency:** The community must share a life that aims at self-sufficiency.

### 2. The Nature of Paternal and Master Justice
The justice of a master over slaves or a father over children differs fundamentally from political justice:
- **Lack of Distinct Otherness:** A slave is considered a "piece of property," and a child (until they come of age and become independent) is considered "just like a part of oneself." 
- **Impossibility of Self-Harm:** Because no one deliberately chooses to harm themselves, there is no "injustice in its simple sense toward things that are one's own." Consequently, true injustice cannot be committed by a father or master against their dependents in the same way it can between independent citizens.
- **Analogy, Not Equality:** While there is a sense of justice in these relationships, it operates by "likeness" rather than being actual political justice, because the necessary condition of equality among distinct individuals is absent.

### 3. Justice in Household Management
Aristotle notes that justice in relation to a wife is more akin to political justice than justice toward children or slaves. However, this is classified under **household management** (economic justice) and remains distinct from the political justice of the wider, public community.

Aristotle bases *political justice* strictly on a relationship between free and equal citizens. Unlike the household where a father or master exerts absolute rule over dependents (who are considered "parts" of him), the political community (*polis*) is made up of distinct, independent individuals sharing a life (*koinōnōn biou*) for the sake of self-sufficiency (*autarkeian*). Without freedom and equality, there is only justice "by likeness," but not true political justice.

## Relationships
- **Law and Equality:** True justice relies on the presence of law, which itself requires individuals to be distinct and equal. When equality is missing, justice is administered differently.
- **Household vs. City:** The distinction maps onto Aristotle's separation of the private sphere (the household, governed by household management) and the public sphere (the city/polis, governed by political justice).

# Natural Justice

## Summary
In Book V, Chapter 7 of the *Nicomachean Ethics*, Aristotle divides political justice (justice within a community of citizens) into two distinct but complementary components: **natural justice** and **legal/conventional justice**. While natural justice has the same power everywhere and is independent of human opinion or decree, conventional justice consists of rules that could initially be decided in multiple ways without moral consequence, but become binding once established by human agreement or law. Aristotle argues against the view that all justice is merely conventional, asserting that even though human affairs are inherently changeable, there is still a justice that exists by nature.

## Key Ideas

- **Two Kinds of Political Justice**:
  - **Natural Justice**: Possesses the same force everywhere, regardless of whether it is recognized, legislated, or agreed upon by people. It is not dependent on human opinion.
  - **Conventional (Legal) Justice**: Pertains to matters that are morally indifferent before a law is made, but which become strictly defined once a community establishes them. Aristotle gives examples such as the exact price of a ransom (one mina) or the specification of a sacrifice (a goat rather than two sheep). 

- **The Argument from Changeability**:
  - A common opinion holds that all justice is merely conventional because the rules of justice vary from city to city, whereas natural phenomena remain constant (e.g., "fire burns both here and among the Persians").
  - Aristotle counters this by acknowledging that while among the gods nothing changes, the realm of human action is inherently characterized by change. Yet, within this changeable realm, there is still a natural order. Just because human laws and customs vary does not mean natural justice doesn't exist.

- **The Analogy of the Right Hand**:
  - Aristotle uses a striking biological analogy: by nature, the right hand is stronger than the left. However, it is possible for everyone to train themselves to become ambidextrous. The fact that the natural state can be altered by human effort, variation, or convention does not negate the existence of the natural baseline itself.

- **The Universality of the Best Constitution**:
  - Just as measuring units for wine and grain differ from place to place depending on local markets, things that are just by human convention differ across various political constitutions. However, Aristotle boldly asserts that "the only one [constitution] that is everywhere according to nature is the best kind."

- **Universals and Particulars**:
  - Aristotle emphasizes that each rule of justice (whether natural or conventional) acts as a universal in relation to the many particular actions that fall under it.

This passage highlights the central tension for those who argue all justice is merely conventional: nature (like fire) operates uniformly everywhere without regard for human borders, whereas what is considered "just" fluctuates wildly from city to city. Aristotle sets up this objection using *akinēton* ("unchangeable," "unmoved") for nature and *kinoumena* ("changing," "moving") for justice, before resolving the tension by proving that even within the fluctuating sphere of human affairs, there remains a natural baseline.

## Relationships
- **Political Justice**: Natural and conventional justice together make up the whole of political justice, which governs relations between free and equal citizens.
- **Decency (Equity)**: Related to the limitations of conventional justice. Since conventional laws must be universal, they sometimes fail to apply fairly to particular cases. Decency acts as a correction to legal justice when its strict universality falls short.
- **Virtue of Character**: Justice, like all virtues, requires a stable, active condition (*hexis*) where one deliberately chooses the mean. Natural justice represents the deepest alignment of this character with human nature.

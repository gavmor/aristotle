# Reciprocity and Exchange

## Summary
In Book V, Chapter 5 of Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics*, reciprocity (or reciprocal exchange) is explored as the essential principle of justice that holds a community (or city) together. Aristotle distinguishes true reciprocity from the simplistic Pythagorean notion of "an eye for an eye" (simple retaliation). In a society characterized by the exchange of disparate goods—such as those produced by a housebuilder and a leatherworker—justice requires a proportionate return rather than strict equality. To achieve this, disproportionate goods must be made commensurable. Human **need** naturally unifies society, but by conventional agreement, **currency (money)** serves as a standardized substitute for need, acting as a middle term to equalize the value of disparate goods and guarantee future exchanges.

## Key Ideas

### Proportionate vs. Simple Reciprocity
Aristotle rejects the Pythagorean definition of justice as simply suffering what one has done (like the justice of Rhadamanthus). Simple reciprocity "sounds the wrong note" in cases involving differing statuses (e.g., an authority figure hitting someone versus a subordinate hitting an authority figure) and does not distinguish between willing and unwilling transactions. True reciprocal exchange is based on *proportionality* rather than strict material equality.

### The Problem of Disproportionate Goods
A community does not arise between two people with identical skills (e.g., two doctors), but between people with different skills (e.g., a doctor and a farmer, or a housebuilder and a leatherworker). The products they create are inherently different and have unequal values. For exchange to happen fairly, these differing products must be equalized. 

### Need as the Foundation of Exchange
The fundamental driver that holds a community together through exchange is mutual **need**. Without need, or without similar needs, people would not engage in exchange. 

### Money as a Middle Term
Because it is impossible to directly compare vastly different goods (like a house and a shoe), there must be a way to make them commensurable. 
- **Currency as Measure:** Currency is introduced by convention (hence its name, related to "current custom") to serve as a mean. It measures all things, including excess and deficiency, allowing us to calculate how many shoes are equal to one house.
- **Currency as Guarantor:** Money also acts as a guarantor for future exchanges. If one does not need anything currently, holding currency ensures that one can acquire what is needed in the future. While the value of money itself can fluctuate, it tends to remain more constant than other commodities.

### Reciprocity Sustains the City
A city stays together by "paying things back proportionately." If people cannot return good for good or evil for evil, the foundation of community dissolves into something akin to slavery. Reciprocal giving and graciousness are vital, symbolized by the placement of the temple of the Graces by the roadway.

This passage illustrates the Pythagorean concept of *antipeponthos* (reciprocity or "suffering in return"), which they equated directly with justice. The justice of Rhadamanthus (a mythical judge of the underworld) embodies this strict retaliation. Aristotle immediately rejects this as a complete definition of justice because it ignores differences in status (e.g. striking a magistrate vs. a private citizen) and intent (voluntary vs. involuntary). Instead, Aristotle introduces his positive thesis: it is proportional return (*antipoiein analogon*), rather than strict identical retaliation, that serves as the binding force of the *polis*.

## Relationships
- **Justice as a Mean:** Like other virtues, justice is a mean. However, justice relates specifically to a *mean quantity* in distribution and exchange, striking a balance between having too much (doing injustice) and having too little (suffering injustice).
- **Distributive and Corrective Justice:** Reciprocity operates differently from both distributive justice (allocating public wealth/honor) and corrective justice (rectifying unbalanced transactions). It is the unique form of justice required for voluntary economic association and trade.

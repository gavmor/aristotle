# Universal Justice

## Summary
In Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* (Book V, Chapters 1-2), Universal Justice is conceptualized as complete virtue in its active exercise toward other people. Unlike particular justice (which deals with fairness, equality, and specific transactions or distributions), universal justice corresponds to lawfulness. Since a rightly framed law commands virtuous actions (such as acts of courage, temperance, and gentleness) and forbids vicious ones, the person who obeys the law exercises complete virtue. Thus, universal justice is not merely a part of virtue, but the whole of virtue, distinguished only by its relational orientation toward the good of another.

## Key Ideas
- **Lawfulness as Complete Virtue:** The law commands citizens to perform virtuous deeds and avoid vicious ones. Therefore, the law-abiding person (the universally just person) embodies complete virtue in practice.
- **Relational Nature:** Universal justice is complete virtue exercised "not simply but in relation to someone else." It is celebrated proverbially ("in justice all virtue is together in one") and is considered the greatest of virtues because applying virtue toward others is a more difficult and noble task than applying it merely toward oneself.
- **"Someone Else's Good":** Justice is uniquely characterized among the virtues as "someone else's good" because it intrinsically benefits another person, whether a ruler or a fellow member of the political community.
- **Distinction from Virtue Itself:** Universal justice and complete virtue are fundamentally the same active condition of the soul; they differ only in their defining essence. Insofar as this condition is an active state of character, it is virtue; insofar as it is directed toward another person, it is justice.
- **Universal Injustice:** Correspondingly, universal injustice (lawbreaking) is not just a specific vice, but rather the entirety of vice as it impacts others.

## Relationships
- **Contrasts with Particular Justice:** While universal justice encompasses all moral virtues through lawfulness, Particular Justice is a specific part of virtue concerned with fairness, equity, and avoiding greed (pleasure from taking more than one's fair share) in distributions and transactions.
- **Relates to Moral Virtue (Arete):** Universal justice is identical to complete moral virtue in substance, with the caveat that it is virtue viewed specifically through the lens of interpersonal relationships.
- **Relates to Politics and Law:** Because the laws of a city aim at the common advantage and prescribe actions conducive to public virtue, universal justice ties individual ethical character directly to the political and legal order of the community.

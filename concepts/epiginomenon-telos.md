# Epiginomenon Ti Telos (Pleasure as Supervening Completion)

In Book X, Chapter 4 of the *Nicomachean Ethics* (Bekker 1174b31–33), Aristotle formulates his definitive account of pleasure, rejecting both the view that pleasure is the ultimate good and the view that it is a physical motion (*kinēsis*) or process of restoration (*genesis*).

## Key Ideas

- **Complete at Every Instant:** Unlike motion (*kinēsis*), which takes time and is incomplete until its end (e.g., building a temple or laying a foundation), seeing and feeling pleasure are **complete at every instant** (*holon*). There is no missing part that must come later to complete its form. ^[extracted]
- **The Bloom on Youth (*Epiginomenon Ti Telos*):** Aristotle asserts that pleasure completes being-at-work (*energeia*) not as an internal component or an external appendage, but as a **supervening end** (*epiginomenon ti telos*)—"like the bloom that comes over those in the flower of youth" (*hoion tois akmaiois hē hōra*). ^[extracted]
- **Conditions of Completion:** Pleasure arises whenever a healthy, unimpeded capacity for sense-perception or intellect operates upon its best and most excellent object. The perfection of the activity naturally evokes and is crowned by pleasure. ^[extracted]
- **Activity-Specific Pleasures:** Because pleasure supervenes directly upon specific activities, pleasures differ in kind according to the activities they complete. A proper pleasure (*oikeia hēdonē*) sharpens and intensifies an activity, while an alien pleasure (*allotria hēdonē*) acts like a distraction and impedes it. ^[extracted]

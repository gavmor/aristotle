# Autarkeia (Self-Sufficiency)

In Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics*, **`αὐτάρκεια`** (*autarkeia*, self-sufficiency) is one of the definitive formal criteria of ultimate human flourishing (*eudaimonia*). 

The word compounds **`αὐτός`** (*autos*, "self") and **`ἀρκέω`** (*arkeō*, "to suffice / to ward off"). Etymologically and philosophically, *autarkeia* means the condition of containing within oneself enough to make life complete and lacking in nothing.

---

## Key Ideas

- **The Formal Definition of Happiness (Book I, 1097b6–21):** Aristotle posits that the ultimate good must be complete (*teleion*) and self-sufficient (*autarkes*). He defines the self-sufficient as **"that which, taken by itself, makes life choiceworthy and lacking in nothing"** (*ho monoumenon haireton poiei ton bion kai mēdenos endeā*). ^[extracted]
- **Non-Solitary Social Self-Sufficiency:** Crucially, Aristotle rejects the Cynic or ascetic view of self-sufficiency as solitary isolation (*bios monōtēs*). Because a human being is by nature a political and social animal (*politikon phusei*), true self-sufficiency for a human being **includes parents, children, a spouse, and friends**, since human flourishing is realized only in community with others. ^[extracted]
- **The Political Dimension (Book V, 1134a26):** In his discussion of justice, Aristotle notes that political justice exists specifically among free and equal citizens sharing a common life (*koinōnōn biou*) aimed at **`αὐτάρκειαν`** (*autarkeian*)—producing the material and moral conditions for a self-sufficient life. ^[extracted]
- **Degrees of Self-Sufficiency (Book X, 1177a27–b1):** In Book X, Aristotle ranks activities by their degree of *autarkeia*:
  - **Moral Virtue (*Praxis*):** Requires external equipment (*organa*)—generosity requires wealth, justice requires transaction partners, courage requires physical strength.
  - **Contemplation (*Theōria*):** Holds the **highest self-sufficiency** (*autarkestātē*). While a wise person still needs basic food and health as a human being, in the act of contemplating itself, they need no external partners or equipment, being able to contemplate even alone. ^[extracted]

# The Contemplative Life (Theoria)

Book X, chapters 6-8, close the Ethics' main argument (before the transitional chapter 9, which pivots to the *Politics*) by asking, in light of everything established about virtue, friendship, and pleasure, what happiness actually *is*. Aristotle's answer is a "way of being-at-work in accordance with the most powerful virtue," and he identifies this as **contemplation** (*theoria*).

## Key Ideas

- **Happiness is not play**: against the view that the leisured pursuits enjoyed by tyrants and the powerful count toward happiness, Aristotle argues play is chosen for the sake of rest, and rest for the sake of being-at-work — "to work hard and undergo troubles all through one's life for the sake of playing" would be foolish and childish. Even a slave can enjoy bodily pleasure as much as the best person, but no one grants a slave a share of happiness, since happiness requires the right *kind* of activity, in accordance with virtue. ^[extracted]
- **The argument for contemplation as complete happiness**:
  1. Happiness should be being-at-work in accordance with the most powerful virtue, belonging to the best part of us — intellect (*nous*), which is "either divine itself or the most divine of the things in us."
  2. Contemplative activity is the **most continuous** (we can contemplate more continuously than we can act), the **most pleasant** (philosophy's pleasures are "wonderful in their purity and stability"), the **most self-sufficient** (a wise person can contemplate alone, whereas just, temperate, and courageous action all require other people to act toward), and the **most loved for its own sake** (nothing comes from contemplating beyond the contemplating itself, unlike action, which always aims at something beyond itself).
  3. Happiness is found "in leisure," and only contemplation is a leisured activity in the relevant sense — political and military virtue's activities are inherently "unleisured," aiming at something beyond themselves (peace, honor, power), whereas contemplation aims at nothing beyond itself. ^[extracted]
- **"One should not think human thoughts, since we are human, but as far as possible be immortal"** — Aristotle explicitly rejects the advice (attributed to poets) to keep to mortal, human-scaled concerns; the intellect, though "small in bulk," "rises much more above everything else in power and worth," and "each person would even seem to be this part" if it is the ruling part — so living according to intellect is living according to one's truest self, not according to something alien. This life "would be greater than what accords with a human being" — divine, insofar as something divine is present in us. ^[extracted]
- **Moral/political virtue is "happy in a secondary way"**: the virtues of character are "human," bound up with feelings and the body, and require external equipment to be exercised (money for generosity, opportunity for temperance, strength for courage) — whereas contemplation "would seem to have little need of external props," even less than virtue of character does; things like wealth and power can positively obstruct contemplation rather than enable it. ^[extracted]
- **The argument from the gods**: since the gods are agreed to be supremely blessed, and it would be "ridiculous" to attribute to them acts of justice (contracts, returning deposits), courage (facing danger), generosity (giving money), or temperance (resisting base desire) — since none of these apply to a being with no needs or dangers — the only activity left for a living, awake god is contemplation. Human happiness, then, extends "as far as contemplation does," which is also why animals (incapable of contemplation) do not participate in happiness at all, however much bodily pleasure they enjoy. ^[extracted]
- **External goods are still needed**, since "nature is not self-sufficient for contemplating" — a body must be healthy and fed — but self-sufficiency "does not consist in excess any more than action does"; Aristotle invokes Solon (happiness belongs to those "supplied with external things in measure... who have lived temperately") and Anaxagoras (the happy person is neither rich nor powerful) as authorities in agreement with this moderate conclusion. ^[extracted]
- **The wise person is "most dear to the gods"**, on the reasoning that if the gods care for human affairs at all, they would delight most in what is best and most akin to themselves (intellect), and reward those who cultivate and honor it most — "so a wise person would be the most happy." ^[extracted]
- **The Ethics ends by denying its own sufficiency**: knowing about virtue is not the same as having it, and "discourses" (arguments) can only reach those already well brought up — reforming people who "live by feeling" requires the compulsory power of law, not persuasion, which is why Aristotle closes by proposing to investigate lawmaking, explicitly framing the *Politics* as the sequel this inquiry hands off to. ^[extracted]

## Open Questions

- Aristotle never fully reconciles the "primary/complete happiness = contemplation" conclusion of Book X with Book I's ergon argument, which derived happiness generally from being-at-work "in accordance with reason" without singling out theoretical intellect specifically — see the parallel open question on [[concepts/eudaimonia]]. Commentators dispute whether this is an "inclusivist" happiness (containing both contemplative and practical elements) read into an "intellectualist" text, or whether Aristotle really does rank contemplation categorically above moral virtue. ^[ambiguous]

*"Happiness, then, is not in play; for it would be absurd for the end to be play, and to labor and suffer hardship one's whole life long for the sake of playing."* This is the sentence the page's first bullet paraphrases: the word for "play," *paidia*, is built directly on *pais*, "child," which is exactly Aristotle's point against the leisured-tyrant view — staking a life's labor on winning a child's activity is a category mistake, since happiness must belong to the best part of an adult.

*"So-called self-sufficiency would belong most of all to the contemplative activity."* This backs the page's second bullet: *autarkeia* builds "enough" (*ark-*, root of *arkeō*, "to suffice") directly onto "self" (*aut-*), and Aristotle is arguing that this trait belongs to contemplation more fully than to any other virtuous activity, since the wise person's own thinking completes itself where just or courageous action structurally needs other people to act toward.

*"One ought not, as those who urge it advise, think human thoughts since one is human, nor mortal thoughts since one is mortal, but as far as possible immortalize oneself, and do everything toward living in accordance with the most sovereign thing in oneself."* This is the sentence behind the page's third bullet. *Athanatizein* coins a verb straight out of *a-* ("not") plus *thanatos* ("death") plus the verb-forming *-izein* — turning immortality from a state one merely has into an activity one does, which matches the page's claim that living by intellect is a practice of reaching toward the divine, not a settled possession of it.

*"So then, if acting is taken away from a living being, and still more if making is, what is left except contemplation? So the being-at-work of a god, surpassing in blessedness, would be contemplative."* This is the sentence the page's fourth bullet draws on: once action and production are stripped from a being with no needs or dangers, *theōria* — built on *theōros*, "spectator, one who looks on," plus the abstract-noun suffix *-ia* — is the only activity left standing, which is exactly how Aristotle argues from what gods can be said to do to what makes human happiness resemble theirs.

*"And Solon, too, perhaps declared the happy well, in saying that they were people moderately furnished with external things, who had done, as he supposed, the noblest deeds, and had lived temperately."* This is the sentence the page's sixth bullet cites as an authority for moderate external goods. *Kechorēgēmenous* is borrowed from the civic institution of the *chorēgia* (root *choros*, "chorus," plus *hēg-*, "to lead," as a perfect passive participle) — where a wealthy sponsor funded a chorus's costumes and training, so external goods, like a chorus's costumes, support the performance without ever being the performance itself.

```
ὁ        δὲ    κατὰ          νοῦν           ἐνεργῶν             καὶ  τοῦτον    θεραπεύων         καὶ  διακείμενος    ἄριστα  καὶ  θεοφιλέστατος      ἔοικεν.
```

*"And the one who is at work according to intellect, and cultivates it, and is disposed best, seems also to be most dear to the gods."* This is the sentence behind the page's seventh bullet: *theophilestatos* compounds *theos* ("god") with the root of *philos* ("dear, beloved") into a single superlative, letting Aristotle close the argument in one word — whatever the gods favor most resembles them most, and the wise person's activity is closest to the gods' own.

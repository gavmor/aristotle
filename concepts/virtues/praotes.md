# Praotes (Gentleness / Good Temper)

**Deficiency:** Spiritlessness (Aorgēsia)
**Excess:** Irascibility (Orgilotēs)

## Summary
**Praotes** (often translated as gentleness or good temper) is the Aristotelian virtue that represents the mean condition concerning anger. A person possessing *praotes* becomes angry at the right things, toward the right people, in the right way, at the right time, and for the right length of time, as reason prescribes. Such a person is undisturbed and not driven purely by passion, erring more on the side of forgiveness than revenge.

## Key Ideas
- **The Proper Measure of Anger:** *Praotes* is not the absence of anger. Rather, it is the appropriate calibration of anger. Aristotle considers it foolish and slavish not to get angry when one or one's loved ones are insulted or wronged. 
- **Guidance by Reason:** The gentle person is not led blindly by emotional passions but responds to situations severely only when and as reason dictates.
- **Inclination Toward Forgiveness:** Although the gentle person experiences appropriate anger, the virtue itself leans slightly toward the deficiency (a lack of anger) rather than the excess, making the person more apt to forgive than to seek harsh retribution.

## Relationships

### The Mean and Extremes
*Praotes* exists as a mean between a deficiency and several forms of excess in relation to anger.

- **The Deficiency (Nameless / Sluggishness):** 
  - A lack of appropriate anger when wronged. 
  - Aristotle characterizes those who exhibit this deficiency as foolish, seeming not to perceive insults, and slavish for overlooking wrongs against themselves or their loved ones.
  
- **The Excess (Irritability / Harshness):**
  - Aristotle identifies several distinct types of excess regarding anger:
    1. **Irritable (Orgiloi):** Quick to anger, at the wrong people and things, but their redeeming quality is that they cool down quickly because they do not hold their anger in.
    2. **Hotheaded (Akrocholoi):** Excessively sharp-tempered and irritable toward everything and everyone.
    3. **Bitter (Pikroi):** They hold onto their rage for a long time and resist letting go until they have exacted revenge. Because they conceal their anger, others cannot help talk them out of it, making them burdensome to themselves and their loved ones.
    4. **Harsh (Chalepoi):** Those who treat others severely on wrong grounds, excessively, and for extended periods without reconciling unless there is punishment. Aristotle notes this is the most common extreme (as humans naturally lean toward revenge) and the most opposed to *praotes*.

### Related Concepts
- **Reason (Logos):** Anger in the gentle person is entirely mediated by reason, demonstrating the harmony between emotion and intellect in the virtuous person.
- **Justice:** By getting angry at the right things and for the right reasons, *praotes* aligns closely with a sense of justice and standing up for what is right, though it differs in its focus on the emotional state of the individual.

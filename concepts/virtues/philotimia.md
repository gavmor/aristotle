# Philotimia

**Deficiency:** Lack of Ambition (Aphilotimia)
**Excess:** Over-ambition (Philotimia)

## Summary

In Book IV, Chapter 4 of Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics*, Aristotle explores an unnamed mean concerned with the proper desire for moderate or small honors. Often referred to in modern translations as "proper ambition" or by the Greek term *Philotimia* (love of honor), this virtue represents the right disposition toward everyday honors, standing in contrast to both an excessive passion for honor and a complete lack of it. Because the mean itself has no established name, the extremes often appear to dispute over the middle territory, leading people to sometimes praise the ambitious person and at other times praise the unambitious person.

## Key Ideas

- **The Unnamed Mean**: Aristotle notes that this active condition of the soul has no specific name. It represents reaching out for honor exactly as one ought—neither too much nor too little, and from the right sources.
- **The Excess (Over-ambition)**: Those who possess an excessive passion for honor are blamed for seeking honor more than they ought, or for seeking it from inappropriate sources.
- **The Deficiency (Lack of ambition)**: Those who lack the passion for honor are blamed for not choosing to gain honor, even as a reward for beautiful and noble acts.
- **Ambiguity of the Extremes**: Because the mean is nameless, the extremes are sometimes praised in different contexts. A passion for honor can sometimes be praised as manly and devoted to the beautiful, while a lack of passion for honor can sometimes be praised as moderate and sensible. 

## Relationships

- **Greatness of Soul (Megalopsuchia)**: Philotimia stands in relation to Greatness of Soul exactly as Generosity stands to Magnificence. While Greatness of Soul is concerned with the right disposition toward *great* honors, Philotimia is concerned with the right disposition toward *moderate* or *small* honors.
- **Generosity and Magnificence**: Aristotle explicitly uses the relationship between Generosity (concerned with everyday financial matters) and Magnificence (concerned with large-scale expenditures) as an analogy to explain the relationship between Philotimia and Greatness of Soul.

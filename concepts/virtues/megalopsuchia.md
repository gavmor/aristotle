# Megalopsuchia (Greatness of Soul)

**Deficiency:** Smallness of Soul (Mikropsuchia)
**Excess:** Vanity (Chaunotēs)

## Summary
In Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* (Book IV, Chapter 3), **Megalopsuchia** (often translated as "greatness of soul", "magnanimity", or "pride") is the virtue of knowing one's own immense worth and acting accordingly. A great-souled person is one who is worthy of the greatest things and accurately assesses themselves as such. It is deeply connected to honor, which is the greatest of external goods. Megalopsuchia is described as an "adornment of the virtues" because it requires the presence of complete virtue to be genuine, and in turn, amplifies their greatness and beauty.

## Key Ideas

- **The Golden Mean of Self-Worth:** Megalopsuchia is a mean with respect to how one evaluates their own worth. The deficiency is being "small-souled" (undervaluing oneself despite being worthy of great things), which Aristotle considers worse and more common than the excess. The excess is "vanity" (considering oneself worthy of great things while actually being unworthy). Thus, the great-souled person is an extreme in the magnitude of their greatness, but represents a mean in correctly and truthfully assessing their worth.
- **Relationship to Honor:** Since honor is the greatest of external goods (even ascribed to the gods), the great-souled person is primarily concerned with it. However, they only accept great honors from serious and virtuous people, recognizing them as what they deserve (or even less than they deserve, as complete virtue has no adequate material reward). They disdain honors from ordinary or random people for minor matters.
- **The Adornment of Virtues:** One cannot be truly great-souled without possessing all other virtues. A person who claims greatness without virtue is merely arrogant or vain. When paired with the other virtues, megalopsuchia acts as their "adornment," making them greater.
- **Demeanor and Actions:** 
  - **Risk-taking:** They do not care for small risks, but will take great risks, even showing disregard for their own life, as life is not worth living on just any terms.
  - **Favors and Reciprocity:** They prefer to bestow favors rather than receive them, and will repay favors with greater ones to maintain a superior position. They are ashamed to have favors done for them.
  - **Social Conduct:** They are highhanded towards the powerful and fortunate, but moderate and polite towards those of middle or lower stations. They are open in their loves and hatreds, speaking truthfully because they do not fear the opinions of others.
  - **Temperament:** They do not marvel at things (as nothing seems truly great to them), do not hold grudges, do not gossip, and do not complain about small necessities. They value beautiful and useless things over productive ones. They possess a slow gait, a deep voice, and a steady, unhurried manner of speaking.

Here, Aristotle logically binds *megalopsuchia* (greatness of soul) to total moral excellence. The word **ἄριστος** (*aristos*, the superlative of good/excellent, from which we get "aristocracy") makes it clear that the great-souled person cannot simply possess one isolated virtue. Because they are worthy of the absolute greatest honors (*tōn megistōn*), they must intrinsically be the best kind of human being, possessing all the virtues in their entirety.

```

Here, Aristotle explains why the great-souled person remains somewhat detached even from the greatest honors bestowed by serious and good people. Even though honor is the proper prize for virtue, *complete virtue* (*aretēs pantelous*) is of such magnitude that no external honor could ever actually equal its worth. The great-souled person accepts it only because others have nothing greater to give, but always recognizes that the external reward falls short of the internal excellence.

\ex ἡ μὲν οὖν μεγαλοψυχία περὶ τιμήν ἐστι μεγάλην, ὥσπερ εἴρηται.
\gl ἡ [hē] [the.NOM.SG]

\ft Therefore greatness of soul is concerning great honor, just as it has been said.
```

The operative word here is **περὶ** (*peri*), which literally means "around," "about," or "concerning." When Aristotle says a virtue is *peri* something, he is establishing its **sphere of operation or domain**, not a psychological obsession or its total definition. It's the same preposition he uses to say courage is *peri* fear and confidence, or temperance is *peri* bodily pleasures. He is saying that great honor is the specific "raw material" or "field of action" in which this virtue exercises itself. The virtue consists in knowing exactly *how* to handle that material.

## Relationships
- **Primary Virtues:** Megalopsuchia requires all other virtues (such as courage, temperance, justice) to be fully realized; it acts as their crown or adornment.
- **Small-souled / Vain:** The two vices representing the deficiency and excess respectively in the assessment of one's own greatness. 
- **Honor:** The external good that megalopsuchia is most concerned with, though the great-souled person ultimately views even honor with a certain detachment, caring only for honor from the virtuous.
- **Generosity and Magnificence:** Just as magnificence is to generosity (handling wealth on a grand scale), megalopsuchia is to the unnamed virtue concerning ordinary honors (handling honor on a grand scale).

# Sophrosune

**Deficiency:** Insensibility (Anaisthēsia)
**Excess:** Dissipation (Akolasia)

## Summary
In Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* (Book III, Chapters 10-12), **Sophrosune** (often translated as temperance or moderation) is the virtue of character concerned with bodily pleasures, specifically those of touch and, to a lesser extent, taste. It is a mean condition between the vices of dissipation (excess) and insensibility (deficiency). The temperate person desires pleasant things that are conducive to health and good condition in a moderate way, guided by right reason, and always for the sake of the beautiful (*to kalon*).

## Key Ideas

*   **Scope of the Virtue**: Sophrosune does not pertain to pleasures of the soul (e.g., love of honor or learning), nor to all bodily pleasures (e.g., sight, hearing, or smell). It is strictly concerned with the animal-like pleasures of touch and taste—primarily eating, drinking, and sexual intercourse.
*   **The Mean Condition**: 
    *   **Deficiency (Insensibility)**: Falling short in the desire for pleasures is considered subhuman and rarely occurs.
    *   **Excess (Dissipation/Profligacy)**: The dissipated person desires all pleasant things, or the most pleasant ones, excessively and prefers them over everything else. This vice is widely reproached because it arises from our animal nature rather than our human nature.
    *   **The Mean (Sophrosune)**: The temperate person finds no pleasure in the things the dissipated person delights in (often finding them disdainful) and is not pained by their absence. They desire what promotes health and fitness moderately, as reason dictates.
*   **Natural vs. Acquired Desires**: Aristotle distinguishes between natural desires (e.g., hunger) where people usually err only in excess (overeating), and specific, cultivated desires where people can err by delighting in the wrong things, or in the right things too much or in the wrong way.
*   **Voluntariness and Habituation**: Dissipation is more voluntary than cowardice because it is driven by pleasure rather than the deranging force of pain. Furthermore, the situations involving pleasure are common, making habituation and the formation of character easier and safer. 
*   **Harmony with Reason**: The word for dissipation in Greek is also applied to spoiled children who live solely by desire. Just as a child must be corrected and live by the instructions of a tutor, the desiring part of the soul in a temperate person must be disciplined, obedient, and in perfect harmony with reason.

## Relationships

*   **Courage (Andreia)**: Often paired with Sophrosune as the other primary virtue of the irrational parts of the soul. While Courage deals with the endurance of pain and fear, Sophrosune deals with the moderation of pleasure. 
*   **The Beautiful (To Kalon)**: Like all virtues, the ultimate aim of the temperate person's choices is the beautiful. The desiring part of the soul looks to this end in harmony with reason.
*   **Pleasure and Pain**: Sophrosune involves a proper relationship to pleasure and pain—feeling pleasure in what is truly good and avoiding the pain that comes from excessive craving or the absence of indulgence.
*   **Right Reason (Orthos Logos)**: The standard by which the temperate person measures how much, in what way, and when to pursue bodily pleasures.

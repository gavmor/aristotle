# Philia (Friendliness)

**Deficiency:** Churlishness (Duskolia)
**Excess:** Obsequiousness / Flattery (Areskeia / Kolakeia)

## Summary
In Book IV, Chapter 6 of the *Nicomachean Ethics*, Aristotle describes the virtue concerning pleasantness in daily life and social interactions. Although he notes that this mean condition has no specific name, he states it most closely resembles *philia* (friendship or friendliness). However, it differs from true friendship because it lacks the passionate emotional attachment or personal affection typically associated with it. The virtue consists in associating with others appropriately—neither seeking to please at all costs nor being unnecessarily contrary—guided by what is beautiful (noble) and advantageous.

## Key Ideas
- **The Nature of the Mean:** *Philia* as a social virtue is the mean condition regarding the pleasure and pain we give to others in our everyday associations. The virtuous person acts properly out of an active, settled condition of character, rather than from emotional attachment. They accept each social situation and person in the way one ought to.
- **Appropriate Flexibility:** The friendly person does not treat everyone identically. They tailor their behavior according to what is fitting for customary associates versus strangers, or people of high position versus regular acquaintances, allotting what is appropriate to each.
- **Guiding Principles:** The person of this character aims to share in others' pleasure and avoid causing pain, but this is always governed by what is "beautiful" and advantageous. They will refuse to join in pleasures that bring disgrace or harm, and they are willing to inflict a little temporary pain if it leads to greater pleasure or good in the long run.
- **The Extremes (Vices):**
  - **Excess:** 
    - *Obsequious:* Someone who constantly tries to be pleasant, agrees to everything, and avoids causing any pain, with no ulterior motive other than the desire to please.
    - *Flatterer:* Someone who exhibits the same overly accommodating behavior but does so for their own advantage, specifically to gain money or what money can buy.
  - **Deficiency:** 
    - *Contrary / Churlish / Hard to get along with:* A person who falls entirely short in social pleasantness, constantly opposing others, ignoring common pleasures, and caring nothing about whether they cause pain to those around them.

## Relationships
- **Friendship (Books VIII & IX):** Social friendliness is the behavioral and character-based counterpart to actual friendship, possessing the same care for pleasant association but without the deep emotional affection and intimate mutual love.
- **Truthfulness and Wit:** Along with Truthfulness (the mean regarding boasting and self-deprecation) and Wit/Tact (the mean regarding humor), Friendliness forms a triad of virtues that govern social interaction, conversation, and community life. 
- **The Beautiful (Kalon):** Like all virtues, friendliness is exercised for the sake of the beautiful. A friendly person will steadfastly refuse to be accommodating if doing so requires participating in something base or disgraceful.

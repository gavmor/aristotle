# Aletheia

**Deficiency:** Self-deprecation / Irony (Eirōneia)
**Excess:** Boastfulness (Alazoneia)

## Summary
In Book IV, Chapter 7 of Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics*, *Aletheia* (Truthfulness) is examined as the virtuous mean concerning self-presentation and how one represents oneself in words and deeds. Crucially, this virtue is not about truthfulness in contractual agreements or matters of justice (which belong to a different virtue), but rather honesty in everyday life, speech, and casual social interactions. The truthful person is one who acknowledges what belongs to them—nothing greater and nothing less.

## Key Ideas

- **The Virtuous Mean**: *Aletheia* is the mean condition regarding self-presentation. The truthful person calls each thing by its right name and is truthful in both life and speech, claiming exactly what belongs to them as their own.
- **The Excess: Bragging (Alazoneia)**: The braggart exaggerates, taking on a reputation for things that do not belong to them, or overstating the things that do. Bragging can be done for no reason (which Aristotle considers empty-headed rather than deeply bad), for reputation, or most disgracefully, for monetary gain (e.g., claiming false expertise in medicine or prophecy).
- **The Deficiency: Irony/Understatement (Eirôneia)**: The ironic person disowns or understates the qualities that belong to them. Aristotle notes that ironic people (such as Socrates) often appear more gracious in character than braggarts because they avoid pretentiousness and do not speak for the sake of gain. However, extreme understatement (like extreme excess) can also become a form of boastfulness.
- **Truth for its Own Sake**: The truthful person is passionately devoted to the truth even when nothing is at stake. Because they love truth and find falsehood inherently base and blameable, they are even more reliable in situations where truth actually matters. 
- **Leaning Toward Understatement**: Aristotle suggests that the truthful person will naturally lean slightly more toward understatement than exaggeration, as this is more harmonious and less grating than being boastful.

## Relationships

- **Contrasts with Justice**: While justice also involves truth and fairness, *aletheia* is specifically concerned with truthfulness in casual speech and life when no legal or contractual matters are at stake.
- **Opposed to Bragging and Irony**: It stands as the mean between the vices of bragging (excess) and irony (deficiency). 
- **Related Virtues in Social Life**: It is one of three mean conditions concerning how we share words and actions with others. While the other two (friendliness and wit/charm) concern providing pleasure in social life and play, *aletheia* is uniquely concerned with truth.

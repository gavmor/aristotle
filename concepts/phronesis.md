# Phronesis (Practical Judgment) and the Intellectual Virtues

Book VI turns from virtue of character to virtue of thought, dividing the rational part of the soul into a **knowing part** (contemplates what cannot be otherwise) and a **calculating/opinion-forming part** (contemplates what can be otherwise, i.e. the domain of action), and identifying five states by which the soul "discloses truth": art (*techne*), knowledge (*episteme*), practical judgment (*phronesis*), wisdom (*sophia*), and intellect (*nous*).

## Key Ideas

- **Art (*techne*)** is a true reasoned state concerned with *making* (production of something other than the activity itself, whose source is in the maker, not the thing made). **Knowledge (*episteme*)** concerns what is necessary, eternal, and demonstrable from prior, better-known sources. Both are rejected as candidates for the master virtue of action: art because it can be exercised well or badly by choice (a deliberately bad artist is, if anything, more skilled), knowledge because action concerns particulars that are "capable of being otherwise," which cannot be demonstrated. ^[extracted]
- **Practical judgment (*phronesis*)** is defined as "a truth-disclosing active condition involving reason about human goods that governs action" — the ability to deliberate well not about a part of one's good (health, strength) but about living well as a whole. Unlike art, "the end of making is different from itself, but the end of action could not be, since acting well is itself the end." Pericles is Aristotle's example of a person of practical judgment. Temperance (*sophrosune*) gets its name, per Aristotle, from "preserving practical judgment" (*sozousa ten phronesin*), since pleasure and pain warp exactly the judgments concerning action, not judgments like geometric theorems. ^[extracted]
- **Wisdom (*sophia*)** is "the most precise kind of knowledge," combining intellect and knowledge directed at the most honorable things by nature — Aristotle explicitly denies wisdom is the same as practical judgment or politics, rejecting the idea that a human being is the highest thing in the cosmos: Anaxagoras and Thales are called wise but not practically judicious, because they "know things that are exceptional and wondrous... but useless," being ignorant of what is advantageous to themselves. ^[extracted]
- **Intellect (*nous*)** grasps the "sources" (first principles) that neither knowledge, art, nor practical judgment can reach by demonstration — it operates "at both extremes": on one side, the unchanging first terms of demonstrations; on the other, the variable ultimate particulars of action, which are grasped by a kind of perception rather than by reasoning (the same faculty by which one "perceives" that a triangle is the simplest possible figure). **Astuteness** (quick comprehension of another's practical judgment) and **thoughtfulness/consideration** (right discrimination of what is decent, close to the discussion of equity in Book V) are related but lesser capacities operating in the same territory. ^[extracted]
- **The central thesis of Book VI (ch. 12-13): virtue of character and practical judgment are mutually dependent and cannot exist without each other.** Virtue makes the end (the target) of action right; practical judgment makes the means to it right. Without virtue, the analogous capacity is mere **cleverness** — the power to achieve whatever end one has set, whether beautiful or base ("we even call people with practical judgment clever and shameless"). Vice "warps someone and makes one be wrong about the sources that govern action," so practical judgment (which needs a correct starting point) is impossible without good character, just as good character's rightness (needing correct means) is impossible without practical judgment. ^[extracted]
- Aristotle credits and partly corrects **Socrates**: Socrates was right that there is no virtue without wise judgment, but wrong to think the virtues simply *are* forms of knowledge — Aristotle's correction is that virtue is an active condition that **involves** right reason (practical judgment), not one that reduces to it. ^[extracted]
- Book VI closes by noting a tension it does not fully resolve: **wisdom (theoretical)** seems to contribute nothing directly to right action, since it is not directed at any coming-into-being — Aristotle's initial answer is that wisdom "produces happiness... by being possessed and being at work," the way health produces health rather than the way medicine produces health, deferring fuller treatment to Book X's account of [[concepts/contemplative-life|contemplation]]. ^[extracted]

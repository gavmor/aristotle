# Predisposition (Dynamis)

In Book II, Chapter 5 of the [[references/nicomachean-ethics]], Aristotle defines predispositions (*dynameis*, singular *dynamis*) as one of the three kinds of things present in the [[concepts/soul|soul]], along with [[concepts/feeling|feelings]] and [[concepts/hexis|active conditions]].

## Key Ideas

- **Definition**: Predispositions are the innate capacities or aptitudes by which we are said to be able to feel things. For example, it is the predisposition in accordance with which we are apt to be angry, to be annoyed, or to feel pity. ^[extracted]
- **Natural inheritance**: Predispositions belong to us by nature before any exercise of them. They are the potentiality that pre-exists actuality. ^[extracted]
- **Virtue is not a predisposition**: Aristotle concludes that virtue cannot be a predisposition. We are neither praised as good nor blamed as bad simply for having the natural capacity to feel emotions. Furthermore, we do not become good or bad by nature, but virtue requires habituation and active choice. ^[extracted]
- **Relation to Hexis**: While predispositions (*dynameis*) name what belongs to us by nature before exercise, active conditions (*hexeis*) name the settled result of having actually exercised those capacities. This mirrors the broader Aristotelian distinction between potentiality and actuality ([[concepts/energeia]]). ^[extracted]

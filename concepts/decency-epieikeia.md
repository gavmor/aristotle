# Decency (Epieikeia)

Bk. V, ch. 10 takes up *epieikeia* — "usually translated in this chapter as 'equity' in its technical legal sense," a footnote in this edition notes, but "this is misleading." Translator Joe Sachs renders it "decency" instead, joining [[concepts/hexis|hexis]], [[concepts/energeia|energeia]], and [[concepts/to-kalon|to kalon]] as another deliberate departure from the standard English tradition: the glossary entry is explicit that Aristotle "is describing something that goes beyond what is equitable (*to ison*)" — decency isn't a technical legal-equity doctrine but one of "Aristotle's most frequent ways of naming human goodness."

## Key Ideas

- **The puzzle decency resolves**: is what's decent the same as what's just, or different? Aristotle says both intuitions are partly right — "the decent thing, though it is better than a certain kind of just thing, is just, and is not better than what is just by being of some other kind." Decency isn't a rival standard to justice; it's a correction *internal* to justice, applied when strict legal justice, precisely because it's stated universally, produces the wrong answer in a particular case. ^[extracted]
- **The reason lies in what a law necessarily is**: "every law is universal, and there are some things about which it is not possible to speak rightly when speaking universally." A law "takes in what applies to the greater number of cases, not failing to recognize the error" — the lawmaker isn't at fault, and neither is the law; "the error is not in the law or in the lawmaker, but in the nature of a particular case," since "the material of which actions consist is that way straight from the start." ^[extracted]
- **The Lesbian ruler**: "a rule for something indeterminate is also indeterminate, as is the lead straightedge used in housebuilding on Lesbos; for the ruler is altered to fit the shape of the stone and does not stay rigid." Decency is this ruler applied to law: not abandoning the law's aim, but bending the letter of it back to what "the lawmaker himself would say if he were there," had he foreseen the case. ^[extracted]
- **A decent person is defined by a specific, almost counterintuitive habit**: someone "inclined to choose and to do things of that sort," who is "not rigidly precise about justice to a fault, but is inclined to take less, even when he has the support of the law." Decency, in other words, shows up as a disposition to *forgo* a strict legal entitlement, not merely as clever legal interpretation. ^[extracted]
- **This sits one level above [[concepts/corrective-justice|the courtroom machinery]] already documented elsewhere**: where corrective justice supplies the mechanism for setting transactions right by an equal, arithmetic mean, decency supplies the standard for when the *letter* of that mechanism should yield to what the case actually calls for — the same "setting straight" language Aristotle uses for corrective justice itself (*diorthoun*) recurs here: decency is called "a setting straight of what is legally just." ^[extracted]

## Greek Gloss

Source: Aristotle, *Ēthika Nikomacheia*, Bk. V, ch. 10 (Bekker 1137a31-b1), Bywater's 1894 Oxford Classical Text, via the [Perseus Digital Library](https://scaife.perseus.org/library/urn:cts:greekLit:tlg0086.tlg010/) (public domain).

> περὶ δὲ ἐπιεικείας καὶ τοῦ ἐπιεικοῦς, πῶς ἔχει ἡ μὲν ἐπιείκεια πρὸς δικαιοσύνην τὸ δʼ ἐπιεικὲς πρὸς τὸ δίκαιον, ἐχόμενόν ἐστιν εἰπεῖν.

**ἐπιείκεια**, morpheme by morpheme:

| ἐπι- | εἰκ- | -εια |
|---|---|---|
| *epi-* | *eik-* | *-eia* |
| "upon, toward" | root related to *eoika*, "to seem, be like, be fitting" (cf. *eikos*, "likely, reasonable, what one would expect") | abstract noun suffix |
| → **ἐπιείκεια**, "fittingness, reasonableness" | | |

The adjective *epieikēs* is built on *eoika* ("to seem, to be like, to be fitting") — the same root that gives Greek its word for what's *probable* or *reasonable to expect* (*eikos*). This is precisely why "equity" misleads: the Latin-derived English word imports a technical legal doctrine (correcting strict law by a separate standard of fairness), while the Greek word's own etymology says nothing about law at all — it says "what fits, what one would reasonably expect a good person to do," which is exactly the wider, character-based sense Sachs's glossary insists on and "decency" preserves.

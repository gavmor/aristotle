# Politeia (Constitution / Polity)

In Aristotle's thought, **`πολιτεία`** (*politeia*) names both the **political constitution** in general—the structural arrangement of civic offices, authority, and citizenship—and the specific true constitution governed by property-owners (often translated as "polity" or "timocracy").

The word is an `-εια` abstract noun derived directly from **`πολίτης`** (*politēs*, "citizen").

---

## Key Ideas

- **The Taxonomy of Constitutions (Book VIII, ch. 10):** Aristotle identifies three true constitutions aimed at the common good, alongside three corruptions (*parekbaseis*) aimed at private gain:
  1. **Kingship (*Basileia*)** $\rightarrow$ corrupts into **Tyranny (*Tyrannis*)**
  2. **Aristocracy (*Aristokratia*)** $\rightarrow$ corrupts into **Oligarchy (*Oligarchia*)**
  3. **Timocracy / Polity (*Timokratia / Politeia*)** $\rightarrow$ corrupts into **Democracy (*Demokratia*)** ^[extracted]
- **Household Analogues:** Each constitution mirrors a specific relationship within the household:
  - Kingship mirrors the paternal relation (father over children).
  - Aristocracy mirrors the marital relation (husband over wife based on merit).
  - Timocracy/Polity mirrors the relation between brothers (equal status by age). ^[extracted]
- **Bridge to Political Science:** In Book X, ch. 9, Aristotle concludes the *Ethics* by asserting that the study of *politeia* and legislation (*nomothetikē*) is required to complete "the philosophy of human affairs" (*hē peri ta anthrōpeia philosophia*). ^[extracted]

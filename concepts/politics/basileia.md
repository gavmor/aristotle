# Basileia (Kingship / Monarchy)

In Book VIII, Chapter 10 of the *Nicomachean Ethics*, **`βασιλεία`** (*basileia*, kingship) is identified as the best and most divine of the three true forms of constitution.

The word is an `-εια` abstract noun derived from **`βασιλεύς`** (*basileus*, "king").

---

## Key Ideas

- **Paternal Character:** Kingship is modeled on the paternal relationship of a father toward his children. A true king cares for his subjects' well-being and acts as a benefactor (*euergetēs*), just as a father provides life and upbringing for his offspring. ^[extracted]
- **Self-Sufficiency of the Ruler:** A true king must be **self-sufficient** (*autarkēs*) and excel in all goods, needing nothing for himself. Because he lacks nothing, he rules not for his own advantage, but purely for the advantage of those he rules. ^[extracted]
- **Corruption into Tyranny:** When a monarch rules for his own private profit rather than the good of the ruled, kingship degenerates into **Tyranny (*Tyrannis*)**, which Aristotle calls the worst of all corruptions. ^[extracted]

# Paideia (Education and Upbringing)

In Ancient Greek thought, **`παιδεία`** (*paideia*) names the complete process of **education, cultural formation, and moral upbringing** of a citizen from youth to maturity.

The word is an `-εια` abstract noun derived from **`παῖς / παιδός`** (*pais / paidos*, "child").

---

## Key Ideas

- **Preparing the Soil:** In Book X, ch. 9, Aristotle asserts that rational instruction (*logos*) cannot move most people unless their character has already been formed by *paideia*. Like ground prepared to nourish seed, a young person's soul must be habituated beforehand to love what is noble (*to kalon*) and hate what is shameful. ^[extracted]
- **Public vs. Private Upbringing:** While public moral education enforced by law (*nomoi*) is best (as in Sparta), where cities neglect public *paideia*, fathers and heads of households must acquire the legislative art (*nomothetikē*) to give tailored private education to their own children. ^[extracted]
- **Habit preceding Reason:** Moral *paideia* begins with emotional and physical habituation long before a child is capable of theoretical understanding. ^[extracted]

# To Kalon (The Beautiful)

Per Joe Sachs's introduction, "the most important word in the whole work" after *hexis* and *[[concepts/energeia|energeia]]*, and the one translators are, in his phrase, "afraid to give you straight" — most render *to kalon* as "the noble" or "the fine," which Sachs argues drains it of its everyday force. His evidence: ordinary English still uses "beautiful" exactly the way Aristotle uses *kalon* — "that was a beautiful thing you did." ^[extracted]

## Key Ideas

- Aristotle states repeatedly and directly that moral virtue's actions are done **for the sake of the beautiful**: courage is "for the sake of the beautiful, for this is the end of virtue" (Bk. III, ch. 8-9); magnificence is "for the sake of the beautiful, for this is common to the virtues" (Bk. IV, ch. 2); the generous person gives "for the sake of the beautiful" (Bk. IV, ch. 1). ^[extracted]
- Aristotle distinguishes **two uses** of *kalon* — bodies and artifacts well-made or well-shaped, and actions well done — which some translators (per Sachs, citing the Loeb edition) treat as unrelated, calling the first merely "aesthetic." Sachs argues Aristotle treats them as one meaning: the person of serious moral stature judges right action "by perception," the same way one perceives that a well-made object could have nothing added or taken from it, and Aristotle explicitly compares virtuous action to a well-made work of art (Bk. II, ch. 6). ^[extracted]
- Of the three highest kinds of good Aristotle names — the pleasant, the beautiful, and the beneficial/advantageous (Bk. I, ch. 8; Bk. II, ch. 3) — the advantageous drops out as merely instrumental, leaving the pleasant and the beautiful as the two things chosen for their own sake (Bk. III, ch. 4). Sachs's introduction argues that across the work as a whole, the beautiful emerges (though never as an explicit thesis) as the primary sense of the good, since even the pleasure that belongs to virtuous action is a "truer pleasure" that is not chosen for its own sake but supervenes on choosing the beautiful. ^[inferred]
- The clearest single statement of the doctrine, per Sachs, comes in the discussion of [[concepts/philia|friendship]] (Bk. IX, ch. 8): "if all people competed for the beautiful, and strained to do the most beautiful things, everything people need in common, and the greatest good for each in particular, would be achieved... for the person of moral stature will forego money, honor, and all the good things people fight over to achieve the beautiful for himself." ^[extracted]
- Sachs's introduction argues that Aristotle treats **justice as failing to reach the beautiful** on its own terms — Book V never once mentions *to kalon* in its discussion of justice — while friendship is explicitly said to involve it throughout Books VIII-IX, which Sachs takes as textual evidence that Aristotle regards friendship, not justice, as completing the account of what the moral virtues are ultimately for. This is Sachs's interpretive argument, not an explicit claim Aristotle states. ^[ambiguous]
- Beyond ethics, Sachs notes Aristotle extends *to kalon* to the cosmological level: in the *Metaphysics* (Bk. XII) the unmoved first mover of the cosmos is said to move all things "in the manner of something loved," and is named, among other things, "the beautiful" — paralleling Diotima's speech in Plato's *Symposium*, where the beautiful is identified with the good itself. ^[extracted]
